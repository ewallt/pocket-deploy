Bunker Hill (1775)

A Costly British Victory

Core Summary

On June 17, 1775, colonial militia fortified Breed's Hill overlooking Boston Harbor. British General William Howe ordered frontal assaults against the entrenched colonials. After two bloody repulses, British regulars finally took the hill on a third charge when colonial ammunition ran low. The British victory came at devastating cost‚Äîover 1,000 casualties out of 2,400 engaged‚Äîdemonstrating that colonial forces, while ultimately driven from the field, could inflict severe losses on professional soldiers.

British Position: After Lexington and Concord, British forces were bottled up in Boston, surrounded by colonial militia encamped on the surrounding hills.
Colonial Plan: Massachusetts Committee of Safety ordered fortification of Charlestown peninsula to threaten British positions and prevent them from controlling the high ground.
Geographic Stakes: Control of Breed's and Bunker hills would determine whether British forces could maintain their hold on Boston or be forced to evacuate.

ü™ñ Forces & Commanders

‚ñº
British: Major General William Howe commanded roughly 2,400 regulars, including elite light infantry and grenadier companies. General Henry Clinton and General John Burgoyne observed from Boston.
Colonial: Colonel William Prescott commanded the Massachusetts militia on Breed's Hill, with support from Colonel John Stark's New Hampshire troops. Approximately 1,500 men actively defended the redoubt.
Casualties: British: 226 killed, 828 wounded. Colonial: 115 killed, 305 wounded, 30 captured. The British casualty rate exceeded 40%.

üìÖ Timeline

‚ñº
June 16, evening: Colonial forces march to Charlestown peninsula. They fortify Breed's Hill (closer to Boston than Bunker Hill) overnight.
June 17, dawn: British ships spot the colonial earthworks and open fire. British commanders plan a direct assault.
3:00 PM: First British assault begins. Colonials hold fire until the British are at close range, then deliver devastating volleys. British retreat in disorder.
3:30 PM: Second assault. Again, colonials repulse the British with heavy casualties.
4:30 PM: Third assault. Colonial ammunition nearly exhausted. British breach the redoubt. Hand-to-hand fighting. Colonials retreat across Charlestown Neck. Dr. Joseph Warren killed during the withdrawal.

‚ö° Turning Points

‚ñº
"Don't Fire Until...": Colonial commanders (traditionally Colonel Prescott or General Israel Putnam) ordered men to hold fire until the British were at point-blank range, maximizing the effectiveness of limited ammunition.
Stark's Rail Fence: Colonel John Stark positioned his New Hampshire men behind a rail fence on the colonial left. They turned back British flanking attempts with disciplined volleys, preventing encirclement.
Ammunition Exhausted: The colonials fought effectively until they ran out of gunpowder and shot. The final British assault succeeded largely because defenders could no longer fire back.

üìä Outcomes

‚ñº
Tactical British Victory: British forces took the hill and held Charlestown peninsula, but at a cost that shocked commanders in London and Boston alike.
Strategic Stalemate: The siege of Boston continued. British forces remained bottled up, and the battle convinced Howe to avoid further frontal assaults on entrenched positions.
Colonial Morale: Despite retreating, colonial forces proved they could stand and fight against British regulars. The lopsided casualty ratio demonstrated that the war would be long and bloody.

üîç Historical Significance

‚ñº

Bunker Hill became a defining moment in the Revolution. The British won the hill but lost any illusions about easily crushing colonial resistance. General Clinton reportedly said, "A few more such victories would have shortly put an end to British dominion in America." The battle's heavy toll shaped British strategy for the rest of the war, making commanders cautious about frontal assaults and contributing to their eventual defeat.

Primary Sources: General Howe's official report, letters from participants including John Chester and Peter Brown, depositions collected by Massachusetts Provincial Congress.
