Galois Theory: Origins and Foundations

Part 2 · Polynomial Equations Through History

The quest to solve polynomial equations spans millennia, beginning with Babylonian clay tablets from around 1800 BCE that record solutions to quadratic problems in geometric form. Ancient mathematicians across cultures—Babylonian, Greek, Indian, Chinese—developed techniques for specific classes of equations, though they lacked the algebraic notation to express general formulas. The Greeks approached equations geometrically, using compass and straightedge constructions to find lengths satisfying quadratic relations. Euclid's Elements contains geometric equivalents of the quadratic formula, solving problems like finding a line segment x such that x(a − x) equals a given area. These early efforts established that quadratics are fundamentally solvable, though in a language quite different from modern algebra.

The general solution to quadratic equations ax² + bx + c = 0, expressed as x = (−b ± √(b² − 4ac))/(2a), became standard in the Islamic Golden Age. Mathematicians like al-Khwarizmi (circa 820 CE) systematically classified quadratics and provided algorithmic solutions, though they avoided negative numbers and treated different cases separately. By the Renaissance, European mathematicians inherited this knowledge and began pushing toward higher degrees. The cubic equation ax³ + bx² + cx + d = 0 proved far more challenging. In the early 16th century, Italian mathematicians Scipione del Ferro, Niccolò Tartaglia, and Gerolamo Cardano developed formulas for cubics, published in Cardano's Ars Magna (1545). These formulas involved cube roots and, surprisingly, sometimes required manipulating square roots of negative numbers even when the final solutions were real—a phenomenon that hinted at the depth beneath polynomial equations.

Cardano's student Lodovico Ferrari extended the methods to quartic equations, also published in Ars Magna. Ferrari's solution reduced quartics to cubics via a clever substitution, demonstrating that quartics are also solvable by radicals. The formulas for cubics and quartics are significantly more complex than the quadratic formula, involving nested radicals and multiple cases. For example, Cardano's formula for x³ + px + q = 0 is x = ∛(−q/2 + √(q²/4 + p³/27)) + ∛(−q/2 − √(q²/4 + p³/27)). These solutions work, but their unwieldiness suggested that extending the pattern to quintics might be hopeless. Mathematicians sought a general quintic formula for over two centuries, trying variations on the techniques that succeeded for lower degrees, yet every approach failed.

The turning point came in the early 19th century when mathematicians shifted from seeking formulas to questioning their existence. Joseph-Louis Lagrange, in his 1770 treatise Réflexions sur la résolution algébrique des équations, analyzed why the known methods worked for quadratics through quartics. Lagrange observed that solving polynomial equations involves studying permutations of roots and constructing auxiliary polynomials (resolvents) whose roots are simpler expressions of the original roots. This insight prefigured group theory, though Lagrange did not formalize the concept. He showed that the solvability of cubics and quartics hinges on particular permutation patterns but could not extend this to quintics, leaving the problem unresolved.

Paolo Ruffini published a proof in 1799 claiming that quintics are generally unsolvable by radicals, but his argument was incomplete and largely ignored. Niels Henrik Abel provided the first rigorous proof in 1824, demonstrating that no formula analogous to those for lower degrees exists for the general quintic x⁵ + ax⁴ + bx³ + cx² + dx + e = 0. Abel's proof used permutation arguments to show that assuming solvability leads to contradictions. While Abel proved impossibility, he did not characterize which specific quintics are solvable; for instance, x⁵ − 1 = 0 has solutions expressible via radicals (the fifth roots of unity), as does x⁵ − x − 1 = 0. Abel's work established that solvability is not universal but depends on the particular equation, raising the question: what distinguishes solvable polynomials from insolvable ones?

Galois answered this question definitively by associating to each polynomial a group of symmetries—permutations of its roots that preserve algebraic relations. The structure of this group determines solvability: a polynomial is solvable by radicals if and only if its Galois group is solvable in the group-theoretic sense. For quadratics, the Galois group is the symmetric group S₂ (or a subgroup), which is solvable. For cubics, the group is typically S₃ or the alternating group A₃, both solvable. Quartics have Galois groups that are solvable subgroups of S₄. However, the generic quintic has Galois group S₅, which is not solvable (it contains A₅, a simple non-abelian group), thus explaining the impossibility of a general quintic formula. Specific quintics with smaller Galois groups, such as x⁵ − 2, remain solvable by radicals.

The historical trajectory from Babylonian geometry to Galois's abstract algebra illustrates a profound shift in mathematical methodology. Early efforts focused on constructing solutions explicitly, treating each equation as a puzzle to be solved by ingenuity. The failure to solve quintics forced mathematicians to adopt a meta-level perspective, asking not "what is the solution?" but "what kind of solutions are possible?" This abstraction—studying structures and their properties rather than specific instances—became a hallmark of modern mathematics. Galois theory exemplifies this shift, transforming the centuries-old problem of polynomial equations into a unifying framework that connects algebra, symmetry, and abstract structures. The impossibility of solving quintics by radicals is not a limitation but an invitation to deeper understanding.
