Trenton (1776)

Washington Crosses the Delaware

Core Summary

On Christmas night 1776, George Washington led 2,400 Continental soldiers across the ice-choked Delaware River to launch a surprise attack on Hessian forces garrisoned in Trenton, New Jersey. The Continental Army overwhelmed the garrison, capturing nearly 900 prisoners and military supplies. Coming after months of devastating defeats, the victory electrified colonial morale and convinced many soldiers to reenlist despite hardship. It proved that Washington could outmaneuver his opponents and that the Revolution could survive its darkest hour.

Desperate Circumstances: Washington's army had retreated across New Jersey after losing New York. Enlistments were expiring, and the Continental cause seemed on the verge of collapse.
British Overconfidence: General Howe had pursued the Continental Army to the Delaware, then dispersed his forces into winter quarters. Hessian troops held outposts across New Jersey, believing Washington incapable of offensive action.
Washington's Gamble: A bold stroke was needed to reverse momentum. Washington planned coordinated attacks across the Delaware, though only his own column succeeded in crossing due to weather and ice.

‚ö° Turning Points

‚ñº
The Crossing: Despite brutal weather, Washington's troops completed the night crossing. Boats piloted by Marblehead fishermen navigated ice floes that could have doomed the operation.
Complete Surprise: Hessian sentries spotted the attack only minutes before Continental troops entered the town. The garrison had no time to form defensive lines.
Artillery Dominance: Captain Alexander Hamilton's and Captain Thomas Forrest's artillery pieces controlled the main streets, preventing organized Hessian resistance and forcing surrender.

üîç Historical Significance

‚ñº

Trenton stands as one of the most consequential surprise attacks in military history. Washington's bold gamble saved the Revolution at its lowest point. The victory demonstrated that American forces could execute complex operations and defeat professional European soldiers. Historian David Hackett Fischer called it "a turning point in a long struggle, not only for American independence but also for the future of human liberty." Emanuel Leutze's iconic 1851 painting cemented the Delaware crossing in American memory as a symbol of determination against overwhelming odds.
