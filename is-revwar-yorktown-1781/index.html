Yorktown (1781)

The Decisive Siege That Won Independence

Core Summary

From September 28 to October 19, 1781, American and French forces besieged General Charles Cornwallis's British army at Yorktown, Virginia. General George Washington commanded approximately 17,000 Continental and French troops. Admiral de Grasse's French fleet controlled the Chesapeake Bay, cutting off British escape and reinforcement by sea. After three weeks of siege warfare, with his fortifications collapsing under allied bombardment and no relief in sight, Cornwallis surrendered his entire 8,000-man army. The surrender effectively ended major combat operations in the Revolutionary War and forced Britain to negotiate American independence. Yorktown stands as the culmination of six years of struggle and one of history's most consequential victories.

ü™ñ Forces & Commanders

‚ñº
Allied: General George Washington (overall command), General Comte de Rochambeau (French ground forces), Marquis de Lafayette (American advance forces). Approximately 8,800 Continentals, 7,800 French troops. Admiral de Grasse commanded the French fleet offshore.
British: General Charles Cornwallis commanded roughly 8,000 British and German troops, including battle-hardened regiments from the Carolina campaigns. No naval support after de Grasse's arrival.
Casualties: Allied: 88 killed, 301 wounded. British: 156 killed, 326 wounded, 7,018 captured (including 840 sailors). An entire British field army surrendered intact.

üìä Outcomes

‚ñº
Britain's Will to Fight Collapses: News of Yorktown reached London in November. Prime Minister Lord North reportedly exclaimed, "Oh God! It is all over!" Parliament soon voted to cease offensive operations in America.
Peace Negotiations Begin: Yorktown convinced Britain that reconquering America was impossible. Negotiations began in Paris, leading to the Treaty of Paris (1783) recognizing American independence.
Franco-American Alliance Vindicated: The victory proved the value of the French alliance. Without French troops, navy, and financial support, American independence would have been far more difficult‚Äîperhaps impossible.

üîç Historical Significance

‚ñº

Yorktown was not just a military victory but the culmination of years of perseverance, strategic vision, and international diplomacy. It demonstrated that the Continental Army had become a professional fighting force capable of coordinated siege operations with European allies. The victory vindicated Washington's patient Fabian strategy, Congress's diplomatic efforts securing the French alliance, and the sacrifices of countless soldiers who had endured years of hardship. Historian Joseph Ellis writes that Yorktown "was the hinge on which American history turned." The surrender led directly to American independence and the creation of the United States as a sovereign nation. Two centuries later, Yorktown remains a symbol of how determination, strategic patience, and allied cooperation can overcome seemingly insurmountable odds.
