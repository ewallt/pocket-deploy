Open Theism: Scriptural Basis and Historical Development

Part 4 · Historical Roots and Precedents in Church History

The development of classical theism's doctrine of divine attributes occurred through centuries of philosophical refinement, heavily influenced by Greek thought. Early church fathers drew on Platonic and Aristotelian categories to articulate God's nature. Divine simplicity, immutability, impassibility, and timelessness became standard features of orthodox theology. Open theists argue this represents Hellenization of biblical faith—imposing alien philosophical categories onto biblical revelation. The God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob experiences temporal succession, emotional response, and relational engagement. Greek philosophical theology, by contrast, described ultimate reality as unchanging, unaffected by temporal events, and emotionally unresponsive. When Augustine, Anselm, and Aquinas synthesized biblical faith with classical philosophy, open theists contend, biblical dynamism was subordinated to philosophical abstraction, requiring reinterpretation of straightforward biblical portrayals as merely metaphorical.

Early church debates about divine foreknowledge and human freedom didn't always resolve in favor of exhaustive foreknowledge. Origen, while affirming God's comprehensive knowledge, struggled with reconciling foreknowledge and freedom, sometimes suggesting God's knowledge of futures involves knowing possibilities rather than certainties. The Cappadocian Fathers emphasized God's transcendence and infinity in ways that might complicate exhaustive foreknowledge of temporal sequences. While none denied God's omniscience, the precise contours of what omniscience entails remained under discussion. It was the medieval synthesis, particularly in Aquinas, that crystallized the doctrine of God foreknowing all future contingents through eternal comprehensive vision. Open theists see this as one possible development, not the only faithful option, and advocate returning to biblical categories rather than assuming medieval conclusions are irreformable.

The Pelagian controversy established that divine grace is necessary for salvation, but it also involved debates about human capacity and divine sovereignty. While Pelagius lost and Augustine's emphasis on grace prevailed, the precise relationship between divine sovereignty and human will remained contested. Semi-Pelagian and later Arminian movements sought to preserve meaningful human freedom against what they saw as deterministic implications in strong Augustinian views. The tension between affirming both divine sovereignty and libertarian freedom runs throughout church history. Open Theism sees itself as resolving this tension by limiting foreknowledge rather than limiting either freedom or sovereignty. Critics charge that the price—denying exhaustive foreknowledge—is too high, severing Open Theism from catholic Christian tradition. Advocates respond that tradition itself contains diverse strands, and faithfulness requires critical engagement, not blind deference.

Medieval debates about divine omnipotence and possibility touched on issues relevant to Open Theism. Can God create a stone too heavy for God to lift? Can God change the past? Can God know future contingents that haven't occurred? These paradoxes generated sophisticated philosophical responses. Aquinas's solution involved distinguishing absolute divine power from ordained divine power, and viewing God as outside time such that all temporal moments are eternally present. Other scholastics explored whether future contingents possess truth values prior to occurrence. William of Ockham defended a strong view of divine freedom and contingency that sometimes seemed to limit what God can know about undetermined futures. While Ockham didn't deny exhaustive foreknowledge, his emphasis on radical contingency created conceptual space later thinkers would explore. Open theists draw selectively from these medieval discussions while rejecting the broader framework of classical theism.

The Reformation brought renewed emphasis on Scripture's authority over tradition and encouraged fresh theological investigation. Luther and Calvin strongly affirmed divine sovereignty and foreknowledge, with Calvin developing double predestination—God foreordains both the saved and the damned. This represented a hardening of deterministic elements already present in Augustine. Arminius and later John Wesley reacted by emphasizing human freedom and responsibility, though still affirming exhaustive divine foreknowledge. The Arminian trajectory created theological space emphasizing relationship, response, and human agency that Open Theism would later develop more radically. Critics argue Open Theism takes Arminianism too far; advocates see it as Arminianism becoming internally consistent by rejecting the uneasy compromise of affirming both libertarian freedom and exhaustive foreknowledge.

Anabaptist and Free Church traditions emphasized believer's baptism, voluntarism in church membership, and the importance of conscious faith commitment. These commitments carried implicit theological freight about human freedom and decision-making. While these groups didn't deny divine foreknowledge, their emphasis on free, uncoerced response to the gospel sat uneasily with deterministic providence. The Baptist insistence that authentic faith must be freely chosen rather than coerced by infant baptism suggested libertarian freedom even when not explicitly theorized. When Open Theism emerged in the late twentieth century, it found particularly receptive audiences in Baptist and Free Church contexts where these theological sensibilities had long existed, even if not articulated through denial of exhaustive foreknowledge. The ecclesial context shaped theological receptivity.

Modern theology introduced new questions about God's relationship to time and history. Schleiermacher, Barth, Brunner, and others reconsidered classical formulations in light of biblical theology movements, historical consciousness, and philosophical developments. Process theology, stemming from Alfred North Whitehead, radically reconceived God as temporal, relational, and limited in power and knowledge. While Open Theism rejects process theology's metaphysical framework and its departures from orthodox Christology and creation doctrine, the process movement demonstrated that questioning classical theism need not mean abandoning Christian faith. Open theists position themselves between classical theism and process theology—more biblical than process theology, more dynamic than classical theism. Whether this middle position is coherent or collapses toward one pole remains hotly debated. The historical trajectory shows that divine attributes have been contested throughout church history, with Open Theism representing one recent development in ongoing conversation.
