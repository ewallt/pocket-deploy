Extreme Value Theorem

Continuous functions on compact sets attain their bounds

Concept Snapshot

The Extreme Value Theorem (EVT) states that a continuous function on a compact set attains both its maximum and minimum values. In ‚Ñù‚Åø, this means continuous functions on closed, bounded sets must achieve both their supremum and infimum at actual points in the domain. This fundamental result guarantees that optimization problems have solutions and underpins much of calculus and analysis.

üí° Key Insights & Intuition

‚ñº
Compactness is Essential. On (0,1), f(x) = 1/x is continuous but has no maximum (approaches ‚àû as x ‚Üí 0‚Å∫). The domain must be closed to prevent "leaking" extrema at boundary points.

Sequential Characterization. The proof shows that compactness allows us to "catch" maximizing sequences before they escape or accumulate outside K.

Optimization. Finding maximum profit, minimum cost, or optimal design parameters reduces to maximizing/minimizing continuous functions on compact feasible regions.

üîÅ Connections & Extensions

‚ñº
Weierstrass Theorem (Approximation). Not to be confused with EVT, but both bear Weierstrass's name and concern continuous functions.
Calculus Application. Combined with Fermat's Theorem, EVT implies: if f is differentiable on (a,b) and continuous on [a,b], critical points plus endpoints suffice to find global extrema.
Topological Generalization. The continuous image of a compact set is compact. Since [m, M] is the continuous image of K under f, and intervals in ‚Ñù are compact iff closed and bounded, f attains its bounds.
