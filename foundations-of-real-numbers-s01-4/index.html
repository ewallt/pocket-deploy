Dedekind Cuts: Carving the Line

Dedekind defined a real number as a partition of ℚ into two nonempty sets A|B such that every a∈A is less than every b∈B and A has no greatest element. Rational numbers appear as “cuts” with a greatest element on the left, while irrationals correspond to cuts where the left set lacks a maximum. In this way, gaps in ℚ become explicit objects. The construction is purely order-theoretic, requiring no metric and revealing the continuum as a completed ordered field.

Arithmetic and order extend by defining operations directly on cuts, then verifying field axioms and order compatibility. This is laborious but clarifying: addition, multiplication, and additive inverses are built so that the embedding of ℚ is faithful and dense. Crucially, the least upper bound property follows from set-theoretic features of cuts, not from geometric pictures. As a bonus, Dedekind’s approach foregrounds order as the heart of real analysis.
