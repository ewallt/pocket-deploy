Open Theism: Criticisms, Implications, and Contemporary Relevance

Part 3 · Practical Implications: Prayer, Trust, and Living with Uncertainty

Prayer takes on profound significance in Open Theist theology. If God faces a genuinely open future and doesn't exhaustively foreknow all outcomes, then petitionary prayer makes robust sense—it genuinely influences God's decisions about how to act. When believers pray for healing, provision, or intervention, they are not merely fulfilling predetermined means to predetermined ends. They are entering into real dialogue with God about how to navigate uncertain futures. God knows what will likely happen with prayer versus without it and responds accordingly. The prayers of the faithful genuinely affect what God decides to do. This makes prayer more meaningful than on classical theism where all outcomes, including prayers and their effects, are eternally fixed. Critics worry this makes God dependent on creatures, but advocates celebrate it as authentic relationship where human agency matters to divine action.

The practice of discerning God's will becomes less about discovering a single predetermined plan and more about wise navigation among multiple good possibilities. Classical theism often encourages seeking "God's perfect will" as a specific path God has ordained. Missing it leads to settling for "God's permissive will." Open Theism suggests God often has preferences but not a single blueprint. Multiple paths might be consistent with God's purposes. Seeking God's guidance involves developing wisdom, considering circumstances, consulting Scripture and community, and making faithful decisions among genuinely open alternatives. This can feel liberating—reducing anxiety about missing "the plan"—or burdensome—losing the security of believing there's a single right answer God knows in advance. The difference shapes pastoral counsel about decision-making in careers, relationships, and life directions.

Trusting God requires different emphases under Open Theism. Believers cannot trust that everything happens according to a comprehensive divine plan, since much happens contrary to God's desires through creaturely free choices. Instead, trust focuses on God's character, power, and commitment. God is trustworthy not because he controls all outcomes but because he is infinitely wise, powerfully engaged, and unfailingly faithful. God promises to work with whatever happens to bring ultimate good, not that he preplanned every tragedy for hidden purposes. When disaster strikes, open theists don't say "God had a reason for this" but "God grieves with us and will work to bring redemption." This might provide more honest comfort to sufferers who struggle with the idea that God specifically allowed their tragedy for some greater good they cannot fathom.

The problem of suffering receives a distinctive Open Theist response. Classical theism typically affirms that God permits each evil for sufficient reason, even if humans cannot discern it. Every horror serves some greater good in God's comprehensive plan. Many find this pastorally difficult—telling a parent whose child was murdered that God allowed it for some hidden purpose seems to implicate God in the evil. Open Theism offers an alternative: much evil results from creaturely free choices that God does not want, does not specifically permit for a reason, and works against. The child's murder is pure tragedy that God grieves over and seeks to redeem, not part of a divine plan. God's power and goodness are preserved by denying that God specifically allows each evil, even if this means acknowledging God takes risks by granting libertarian freedom.

Living with uncertainty becomes a spiritual discipline. If the future is genuinely open, believers must cultivate trust in God's present faithfulness rather than confidence in exhaustive foreknowledge. The future contains real possibilities for both good and ill. God's promises are reliable—Christ will return, justice will prevail, God's kingdom will come—but the path remains partially uncertain. Believers are called to faithfulness in present decisions, trusting that God will accomplish purposes through wise engagement with whatever unfolds. This can produce anxiety for those who want assurance that everything is under control. It can produce liberation for those who felt trapped by determinism. The pastoral question is whether uncertainty acknowledged is healthier than uncertainty denied by assertion of comprehensive divine control that doesn't match lived experience.

Evangelism and mission gain urgency if Open Theism is true. Outcomes genuinely depend on human response and effort. Whether people hear the gospel depends partly on whether believers witness. Whether they respond depends on their free choices. The scope of salvation depends on how many freely accept God's grace. God desires all to be saved but cannot guarantee it without overriding freedom. This makes the church's mission desperately important—human participation in evangelism makes real difference to what actually occurs, not merely to fulfilling predetermined means. Critics worry this makes salvation depend too much on human effort, approaching Pelagianism. Advocates argue it simply takes seriously the biblical commands to evangelize and the genuine possibility of human rejection. Mission matters because outcomes are not yet fixed.

Corporate worship and spiritual formation develop differently when Open Theism shapes piety. Confession becomes more straightforward—believers can confess genuine mistakes, wrong choices, paths they wish they hadn't taken, rather than needing to reframe all decisions as parts of God's ordained plan. Lament gets fuller expression—believers can honestly grieve tragedies as evils God also opposes rather than needing to find divine purpose in them. Gratitude focuses on God's present goodness and faithful response to circumstances rather than thanking God for ordaining all events including painful ones. Hymns and liturgies emphasizing God's comprehensive control sit uneasily with Open Theist conviction, while those emphasizing God's responsive love, powerful engagement, and ultimate faithfulness resonate more naturally. The practical question is whether Open Theist spirituality produces healthier Christian formation or undermines necessary confidence in divine providence.
