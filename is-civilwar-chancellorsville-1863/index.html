Chancellorsville (1863)

Lee's Masterpiece, Jackson's Last Battle

Core Summary

In May 1863, Robert E. Lee faced Joseph Hooker's 134,000-man Army of the Potomac with only 60,000 troops. Lee audaciously split his army, sending Stonewall Jackson on a flanking march that crushed the Union right flank. Despite being outnumbered 2-to-1, Confederate tactical brilliance won a stunning victory. But the triumph came at terrible cost: Jackson was mortally wounded by friendly fire. Lee called Chancellorsville his 'perfect battle,' yet losing Jackson left a void Confederate command never filled. The victory emboldened Lee to invade Pennsylvania, leading to Gettysburg.

Hooker's Plan: After Burnside's disaster at Fredericksburg, 'Fighting Joe' Hooker rebuilt Army morale and planned a brilliant maneuver to flank Lee out of his Rappahannock defenses.
Lee's Gamble: Rather than retreat, Lee divided his outnumbered army. He left 10,000 under Jubal Early to hold Fredericksburg while taking 43,000 west to confront Hooker's main force.
Jackson's March: Lee divided his army again, sending Jackson's 28,000 troops on a 12-mile flanking march through the Wilderness to hit Hooker's exposed right flank.

Union: Hooker commanded 133,868 troops. After initial success, he lost his nerve and adopted defensive posture despite numerical superiority.

Casualties: Union: 1,606 killed, 9,762 wounded, 5,919 missing. Confederate: 1,665 killed, 9,081 wounded, 2,018 missing. Total: approximately 30,051 casualties.

üìÖ Timeline

‚ñº
May 1: Hooker crosses Rappahannock and concentrates at Chancellorsville. Initial success turns to caution. Lee marches to meet him.
May 2: Jackson's corps completes flanking march undetected. At 5:15 PM, they explode from the woods, routing Oliver O. Howard's XI Corps. Jackson is shot by his own men while scouting after dark.
May 3: Stuart leads furious attacks that reunite Lee's divided army. Hooker is stunned by cannon fire. Union line contracts.
May 4: Lee defeats Union forces at Salem Church. Hooker, demoralized, orders retreat across Rappahannock despite still outnumbering Lee 2-to-1.
May 10: Jackson dies from pneumonia following amputation of his shattered arm. Lee laments: 'I have lost my right arm.'

‚ö° Turning Points

‚ñº
Hooker's Loss of Nerve: Despite superior position and numbers, Hooker adopted defensive posture, surrendering initiative to Lee. His subordinates were stunned.
Jackson's Flank Attack: The surprise attack at dusk shattered XI Corps and unhinged Hooker's entire position. Jackson's aggressiveness created opportunities Lee exploited brilliantly.
Jackson's Wounding: Mortally wounded by North Carolina troops in darkness, Jackson's loss transformed Confederate victory into pyrrhic triumph. Lee never found another corps commander matching Jackson's audacity.

üìä Outcomes

‚ñº
Confederate Tactical Masterpiece: Lee's audacious risk-taking produced the Civil War's most lopsided Confederate victory proportional to forces engaged. Military historians study it as perfect battle execution.
Strategic Emptiness: Despite brilliant victory, Lee gained no strategic advantage. Union army escaped intact. Within two months, Lee invaded Pennsylvania seeking decisive battle he never achieved at Chancellorsville.
Irreplaceable Loss: Jackson's death deprived Lee of his most aggressive subordinate. At Gettysburg, Lee sorely missed Jackson's instinct for the jugular. No Confederate general matched his combination of religious zeal and tactical daring.

üîç Historical Significance

‚ñº

Chancellorsville represents Robert E. Lee at his zenith. Historian Shelby Foote called it 'Lee's greatest battle,' while Stephen Sears deemed it 'the most remarkable of Lee's victories.' Outnumbered more than 2-to-1, Lee violated every military maxim by dividing his army not once but twice in the enemy's presence‚Äîand won decisively. Yet strategic barrenness haunted the triumph. Lee's army suffered 22% casualties it could not replace. Jackson's death created a command vacuum Lee never filled. He reorganized the Army of Northern Virginia from two corps into three, promoting Richard Ewell and A.P. Hill. Neither approached Jackson's aggressive brilliance. At Gettysburg, Lee's orders to Ewell lacked the crisp certainty Jackson would have executed without question. Chancellorsville's lessons were contradictory: it proved Lee's tactical genius while obscuring his strategic limitations. Victory's ease encouraged the Gettysburg invasion. Losing Jackson ensured that invasion's failure.
