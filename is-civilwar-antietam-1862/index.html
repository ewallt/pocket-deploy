Antietam (1862)

America's Bloodiest Single Day

Core Summary

On September 17, 1862, General George B. McClellan's Army of the Potomac attacked Robert E. Lee's Army of Northern Virginia near Sharpsburg, Maryland. Despite possessing Lee's battle plans, McClellan launched piecemeal attacks that Lee parried with desperate counterstrokes. Savage fighting at the Cornfield, Bloody Lane, and Burnside's Bridge produced over 22,000 casualties in a single day‚Äîmore than any other day in American military history. Though tactically a draw, Lee's retreat ended his Maryland campaign. The battle gave Lincoln the victory he needed to issue the Emancipation Proclamation, transforming the war's purpose from Union preservation to ending slavery.

Lee's Invasion: After victories at Second Bull Run, Lee invaded Maryland hoping to win European recognition, influence Northern elections, and threaten Washington. He expected Maryland to rise for the Confederacy.
Special Order 191: A Union soldier discovered Lee's wrapped cigars containing Lee's entire campaign plan. McClellan knew Lee had divided his army but moved cautiously, squandering the advantage.
Converging Armies: Lee concentrated his scattered forces near Sharpsburg with the Potomac River at his back‚Äîa desperate position. McClellan had 75,000 troops versus Lee's 38,000. Victory seemed certain.

ü™ñ Forces & Commanders

‚ñº
Union: McClellan commanded approximately 75,000 troops. His corps commanders‚ÄîBurnside, Sumner, Hooker‚Äîattacked piecemeal rather than coordinating. McClellan held 20,000 reserves he never committed.
Confederate: Lee commanded 38,000 troops stretched thin across a three-mile line. Jackson held the left, Longstreet the center and right. Every regiment was committed; Lee had no reserves.
Casualties: Union: 2,108 killed, 9,540 wounded, 753 missing. Confederate: 1,546 killed, 7,752 wounded, 1,018 missing. Total: approximately 22,717 casualties in 12 hours of fighting.

üìÖ Timeline

‚ñº
September 17, dawn: Hooker's corps attacks through the Cornfield. Savage fighting surges back and forth. Every stalk of corn in a 30-acre field is cut down by bullets. Thousands fall in minutes.
September 17, mid-morning: Sumner's corps attacks the Confederate center at the Sunken Road (Bloody Lane). Union troops pour fire into the trapped Confederates. Bodies pile four deep.
September 17, afternoon: Burnside finally crosses Antietam Creek after hours of delay. His corps threatens Lee's right flank and route of retreat. Confederate collapse seems imminent.
September 17, late afternoon: A.P. Hill's division arrives from Harpers Ferry after a forced march. They slam into Burnside's exposed flank, driving Union forces back to the bridge.
September 18: Both armies stand bloodied but intact. Lee dares McClellan to attack again. McClellan refuses. That night, Lee retreats across the Potomac. The invasion has failed.

‚ö° Turning Points

‚ñº
The Cornfield: Six hours of fighting for a single cornfield produced 13,000 casualties. Neither side gained advantage, but Confederate losses proportionally hurt Lee more. His army was bled white.
Bloody Lane Breakthrough: When Union troops flanked the Sunken Road, Confederate defenders fled in panic. A gap opened in Lee's center. McClellan had 20,000 fresh reserves but refused to commit them.
Hill's Last-Minute Arrival: A.P. Hill's men, wearing captured Union blue overcoats, approached Burnside's flank unrecognized. Their surprise attack saved Lee's army from destruction and retreat route from capture.

üìä Outcomes

‚ñº
Tactical Draw, Strategic Victory: Neither side achieved tactical breakthrough. But Lee's retreat into Virginia ended the Maryland Campaign and destroyed Southern hopes of European intervention.
Emancipation Proclamation: Lincoln had waited for a victory to issue the proclamation. Antietam, though hardly decisive, gave him political cover. On September 22, he announced enslaved persons in rebel states would be free on January 1, 1863.
McClellan's Failure: Despite superior numbers and Lee's desperate position, McClellan failed to destroy the Confederate army. Lincoln's frustration grew. In November, he relieved McClellan of command.

üîç Historical Significance

‚ñº

Antietam stands as the Civil War's pivotal moment. Historian James McPherson calls it 'the most important single day of the war.' Lee's retreat preserved the Union by preventing European recognition of the Confederacy. The Emancipation Proclamation transformed the war from a limited conflict to a revolutionary struggle. Britain and France, both having abolished slavery, could not support the South after Lincoln redefined the war's purpose. The battle's terrible casualties‚Äî22,000 in one day‚Äîforeshadowed the war's industrial-scale killing. Photographer Alexander Gardner's images of corpses strewn across the battlefield brought war's horror to the Northern public. The battle proved Lee could be stopped and his army was not invincible. Yet McClellan's failure to pursue showed the Army of the Potomac still lacked killer instinct.
