Calvary — Part 1: The Place of a Skull

On a hill outside the city, shameful instruments become the stage where Scripture and mercy converge.

Golgotha is not chosen for beauty. It is public, ugly, efficient—a warning to passersby. Jesus arrives already weakened, with Simon of Cyrene pressed into service along the way. The soldiers offer wine mingled with gall; He tastes and refuses, choosing clear obedience over numbed endurance. The place tells a story about Rome’s power and human contempt, yet it will soon tell another: about a kingdom whose strength is self-giving and whose victory is truth revealed under pressure.

“There they crucified Him.” The sentence is spare because the reality is not. Nails fasten innocence to wood; a beam lifts between thieves. Below, garments are divided by lot, and above, a title is nailed in three languages. None of it is accidental drama. Prophecy had drawn these contours long before—numbered with transgressors, lots cast, bones unbroken—and the hour now colors within those lines. The Scriptures do not coerce the scene; they interpret it.

The sign reads, “Jesus of Nazareth, the King of the Jews.” Meant as a jab at priests and a placard for Rome, it becomes an unintended confession. The King reigns from a height where kings do not climb. He refuses to save Himself so He can save others, declining every taunt that would have turned miracle into self-protection. The mockers cannot imagine a power that would rather heal enemies than humiliate them. Calvary will teach them, and us, another grammar for greatness.

At the foot of the cross, calculations continue. Priests congratulate themselves; soldiers watch for trouble; curious citizens tally rumors against what they see. The disciples, scattered by fear, hover at the margins. Only a few faithful stand near—women who have learned to stay where pain concentrates. Their quiet companionship keeps a human circle around the Holy One who is being dehumanized. In their presence, the world is less cruel than it might have been.

The hill gathers voices—the jeer of the crowd, the legal Latin of the prefect, the whisper of Scripture remembered, the breath-prayers of the faithful. Jesus answers not with thunder but with patience. The silence is not emptiness; it is endurance doing its work. The cross is becoming a pulpit, and every gesture is part of its sermon: refusal of anesthetic, acceptance of shame, regard for the small. The King is teaching without a classroom.

Nothing here is wasted. The wood Rome meant for terror becomes a tree of life; the hill meant for deterrence becomes a doorway for the nations. The chapter slows us down at the site so we will not skip to endings without learning the method: love does not dominate; it gives. Before any curtain tears or earth quakes, Calvary already reveals what kind of God is with us—a God who suffers our violence without returning it and keeps His promises while we break ours.

Source: Ellen G. White, The Desire of Ages, Chapter 78 “Calvary”.
