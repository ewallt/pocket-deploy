      Prehistory and Lutetia

      Long before monuments and boulevards, the Paris Basin’s rivers supported small settlements that traded along the Seine. When the Romans conquered Gaul, they founded Lutetia on the Île de la Cité and the Left Bank, laying streets, baths, and an amphitheater that anchored a regional hub.

      From Fishermen to Romans

      The Parisii tribe controlled river traffic until Roman engineers imposed a grid, bridges, and civic buildings; fragments like the Arènes de Lutèce and Cluny baths still hint at that first urban plan.

      Medieval Paris

      After Rome’s decline, Paris reemerged as a royal and religious center, clustering around the cathedral and palace on the island and spilling to the banks. Guilds, markets, and the University of Paris turned it into a magnet for learning and trade.

      The Island City

      Notre-Dame’s Gothic rise symbolized both piety and power, while walled precincts protected a dense maze of streets, convents, and workshops.

      Schools and Scriptoria

      Scholastics on the Left Bank shaped Europe’s intellectual life, and bookmaking flourished around the Latin Quarter’s colleges and cloisters.

      Renaissance and Enlightenment

      Kings invited Italian artists, rebuilt palaces, and opened grand squares, while printers and salons spread humanist and later Enlightenment ideas. Paris became both a stage for courtly spectacle and an engine of critique.

      Streets, Squares, and Print

      New bridges knit Right and Left Banks; the Place des Vosges set a model for planned urban space; presses multiplied pamphlets that traveled faster than royal censors.

      Revolution and Haussmann

      Revolutionary crowds made the city a theater of politics, toppling symbols and redrawing institutions; decades later, Baron Haussmann’s works remade the fabric with boulevards, parks, and modern sewers.

      Urban Upheaval

      From 1789 through the 19th century, barricades, regimes, and reforms recurred; wide avenues, uniform façades, and rail hubs then oriented Paris to industry and spectacle.

      Belle Époque to WWII

      World’s fairs, the Métro, electricity, and avant-garde art defined a glittering Belle Époque even as social tensions simmered; war, occupation, and liberation brought rupture and resilience.

      Modernity and Crisis

      Cafés and studios incubated movements from Impressionism to Surrealism; 20th-century conflicts scarred the city yet also accelerated infrastructure and planning.

      Postwar to Present

      Reconstruction, immigration, and suburban growth produced a polycentric metropolis, while preservation laws defended historic cores. Today Paris balances global tourism and culture with climate goals, mobility shifts, and social equity.

      Global City

      Projects from the périphérique to Grand Paris Express, pedestrian riverbanks, and bike networks reflect a long arc: a river town turned world city still reorganizing itself around people, public space, and transit.
