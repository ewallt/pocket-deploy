Open Theism: Core Tenets and Philosophical Foundations

Part 4 · Biblical Foundations of Open Theism

Open theists ground their theology in biblical texts that depict God as responsive, relational, and temporally engaged with creation. Passages where God "changes his mind" provide key evidence. When God says through Jeremiah "If that nation I warned repents of its evil, then I will relent" (Jeremiah 18:8), open theists see genuine divine responsiveness to human choices. The story of Nineveh's repentance and God's decision not to destroy the city (Jonah 3) demonstrates that human actions can alter divine intentions. These texts appear throughout Scripture—God relenting from judgment after Moses's intercession (Exodus 32), God expressing regret over making Saul king (1 Samuel 15:11), God declaring surprise at Israel's actions (Jeremiah 3:7). Classical theism typically interprets these as anthropomorphic accommodations to human understanding, but open theists argue the cumulative weight suggests they reveal something true about God's actual engagement with time.

Texts describing God "testing" or "learning" support the Open Theist reading. When God tests Abraham by commanding him to sacrifice Isaac, then says "Now I know that you fear God" (Genesis 22:12), the language suggests God gained knowledge through the event. When God tells Moses "I will test them and see whether they will follow my instructions" (Exodus 16:4), the text implies God uses temporal processes to discover outcomes. Critics respond that an omniscient God need not literally "learn," and testing serves purposes other than divine knowledge acquisition—it reveals character to the tested person or to others. Open theists counter that the most natural reading takes these statements at face value, and hermeneutical principles should not force figurative interpretations merely to preserve philosophical assumptions about divine immutability and foreknowledge.

The biblical portrayal of prayer as genuinely influencing God strengthens the Open Theist case. Abraham's intercession for Sodom (Genesis 18) involves real bargaining that affects God's decision-making. Hezekiah's prayer leads God to add fifteen years to his life (2 Kings 20). James declares "The prayer of a righteous person is powerful and effective" (James 5:16), suggesting prayer makes a real difference. If God already knows and has decided all outcomes eternally, open theists ask, how can prayer be "effective" in the sense of accomplishing what would not otherwise occur? Classical theists respond that God ordains both the means (prayer) and the ends (outcomes) together, so prayer genuinely causes effects within God's eternal plan. Open theists find this unsatisfying, arguing it makes prayer more theatrical than authentic, a predetermined means to a predetermined end rather than genuine communication that influences decision-making.

Prophecy presents challenges for Open Theism that require careful handling. Biblical predictions that come to pass might seem to require exhaustive foreknowledge. Open theists offer several responses. First, many prophecies are conditional—dependent on whether people respond with obedience or rebellion. Second, some prophecies concern events God intends to bring about directly through divine action rather than through human free choices. Third, prophecies might be probabilistic rather than certain, based on God's perfect knowledge of likely outcomes. Fourth, apparent prophecies might be later theological reflections read back into narratives rather than literal predictions made in advance. Critics find these responses inadequate, pointing to detailed Messianic prophecies and specific predictions (like Cyrus's name in Isaiah 44:28) as requiring foreknowledge of free choices. Open theists reply that divine foreknowledge could extend to particular individuals without being exhaustive of all futures.

The character of God as described in Scripture, open theists argue, aligns better with their view than with classical immutability and impassibility. The God of the Bible experiences emotions, responds to events, and changes in relation to creation. God grieves over human wickedness (Genesis 6:6), experiences pleasure in faithful servants, grows angry at injustice, and shows compassion that leads to altered plans. The prophets portray God as a passionate lover whose heart breaks over Israel's unfaithfulness. Jesus weeps over Jerusalem and agonizes in Gethsemane. Open theists see these as revealing God's genuine emotional life rather than mere metaphors. Classical theism's emphasis on divine impassibility—God's immunity to emotional change or suffering—seems to require viewing much biblical language as not literally true of God, creating tension between philosophical theology and scriptural narrative.

The concept of divine risk-taking appears in Jesus's parables and teachings. The parable of the sower (Matthew 13) depicts God as scattering seed that may or may not take root depending on soil conditions—a metaphor suggesting genuine uncertainty about outcomes. The parable of the prodigal son shows a father who grants freedom that might lead to ruin, then genuinely rejoices when the son returns in a way that suggests the outcome was not certain from the beginning. Jesus's lament "O Jerusalem, Jerusalem, you who kill the prophets and stone those sent to you, how often I have longed to gather your children together, as a hen gathers her chicks under her wings, and you were not willing" (Matthew 23:37) expresses divine desire frustrated by human refusal. This language of longing and frustration makes most sense if God faces genuine possibilities, not predetermined certainties.

Open theists acknowledge that other biblical texts seem to support exhaustive foreknowledge and meticulous providence. Isaiah declares "I make known the end from the beginning, from ancient times, what is still to come" (Isaiah 46:10). Paul speaks of predestination and God's foreknowledge (Romans 8:29-30). Ephesians describes God as "him who works out everything in conformity with the purpose of his will" (Ephesians 1:11). Open theists interpret these texts as emphasizing God's sovereign purposes and ability to bring them about without requiring that every detail is specifically foreordained. They see the biblical witness as complex, requiring careful interpretation that honors both divine sovereignty texts and divine responsiveness texts rather than collapsing one set into the other. The debate centers on which hermeneutical framework best integrates all the biblical data.
