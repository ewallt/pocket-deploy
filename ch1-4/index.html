Gethsemane (Chapter 74)

The world’s crisis gathers in a garden where the sinless One bears the sinner’s load—alone.

Under a cloudless Passover moon, Jesus turns from consoling speech to a silence heavy with sorrow. He has walked in the light of the Father’s presence all His days, but now He enters darkness, numbered with transgressors. Gethsemane reveals atonement from within the human will. Temptation assails Him to abandon the cup, to choose self-preservation over self-surrender. Yet where Adam grasped, the Second Adam yields: “Not as I will, but as Thou wilt.” Obedience reaches its deepest truth here—not mere compliance, but trust in a love that feels absent. The disciples, bewildered and weary, sleep within earshot of history. Compassion marks Jesus’ rebukes; even in extremity He thinks of them. The contrast underscores the solitude of redemption: friendship is near, but fellowship in this agony is impossible. He treads the winepress alone. Prayer shapes the battle. Three times He pleads, three times He yields, and the will hardens into holy resolve. The garden answers the serpent’s lie with lived truth: the Son trusts the Father even when the Father’s face is hidden. Love proves stronger than dread.
