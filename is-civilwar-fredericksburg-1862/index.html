Fredericksburg (1862)

Futile Courage Against Stone Wall

Core Summary

On December 13, 1862, General Ambrose Burnside ordered the Army of the Potomac to assault Lee's fortified positions on the heights behind Fredericksburg, Virginia. Wave after wave of Union infantry charged across open ground toward Confederate troops protected by a stone wall and sunken road. Longstreet's artillery and rifle fire slaughtered attackers with mechanical efficiency. Burnside launched 14 separate assaults; all failed. Over 12,000 Union casualties bought no tactical gain. Lee, watching the carnage, reportedly said: 'It is well that war is so terrible, otherwise we should grow too fond of it.' The disaster destroyed Burnside's credibility and Northern morale plummeted to its lowest point.

‚ñº
Lincoln's Impatience: After Antietam, McClellan refused to pursue Lee. Lincoln relieved him and appointed Burnside, who protested he was unqualified. Lincoln insisted the Army of the Potomac must move before winter.
Race to Fredericksburg: Burnside marched swiftly toward Fredericksburg, planning to cross the Rappahannock River before Lee could concentrate. Pontoon bridges arrived late. The delay gave Lee time to fortify the heights.
Burnside's Dilemma: With Lee dug in on commanding terrain, Burnside should have withdrawn or flanked. Political pressure and personal pride drove him to attack the strongest position in Virginia.

ü™ñ Forces & Commanders

‚ñº
Union: Burnside commanded 114,000 troops organized in three Grand Divisions. Sumner and Franklin commanded the assault forces. Burnside's coordination was poor; attacks were piecemeal and poorly supported.
Confederate: Lee commanded 72,500 troops perfectly positioned. Longstreet held Marye's Heights and the stone wall. Jackson anchored the right. Confederate artillery dominated the battlefield.
Casualties: Union: 1,284 killed, 9,600 wounded, 1,769 missing. Confederate: 595 killed, 4,061 wounded, 653 missing. Total: approximately 17,962 casualties, the vast majority Union.

üìÖ Timeline

‚ñº
December 11: Union engineers lay pontoon bridges under Confederate sniper fire. Federal artillery bombards Fredericksburg. Union troops cross and occupy the town.
December 12: Lee completes defensive preparations. Confederate troops hold nearly impregnable positions on Marye's Heights with clear fields of fire. Lee invites attack.
December 13, morning: Franklin's Left Grand Division attacks Jackson's position. Initial success collapses when Confederate counterattacks drive Union forces back.
December 13, afternoon: Sumner's Right Grand Division assaults Marye's Heights. Brigade after brigade charges across open ground toward the stone wall. All are repulsed with terrible loss.
December 13, evening: Burnside orders continued attacks despite obvious futility. Officers beg him to stop. Finally, darkness ends the slaughter. Wounded freeze on the battlefield overnight.

‚ö° Turning Points

‚ñº
The Stone Wall: Confederate infantry lined four-deep behind a stone wall at the base of Marye's Heights fired devastating volleys. Union troops who reached within 100 yards were mowed down. The position was impregnable to frontal assault.
Meade's Breakthrough: George Meade's division briefly penetrated Jackson's line, creating an opportunity. Franklin failed to support or exploit the breakthrough. Confederate reserves sealed the gap.
Burnside's Obsession: Despite catastrophic losses, Burnside planned to personally lead his old IX Corps in a final assault. Subordinates convinced him to cancel. One more attack would have annihilated the corps.

Complete Confederate Victory: Lee inflicted 3:1 casualties without tactical risk. Burnside's army was thoroughly beaten. The Army of Northern Virginia's morale soared; Fredericksburg seemed proof of Confederate invincibility.
Northern Despair: News of the disaster crushed Northern morale. Lincoln reportedly said: 'If there is a worse place than Hell, I am in it.' Calls for negotiated peace intensified.

üîç Historical Significance

‚ñº

Fredericksburg stands as one of history's most one-sided battles. Historian Shelby Foote called it 'the most lopsided Confederate victory of the war.' The battle demonstrated the futility of frontal assaults against entrenched defenders‚Äîa lesson too often forgotten in later battles. Lee's perfect defensive victory strengthened his reputation for invincibility and emboldened him to launch the Gettysburg campaign. For the Union, Fredericksburg represented the Army of the Potomac's nadir. Soldiers' faith in their commanders evaporated. Yet the army's courage under impossible conditions proved its fighting spirit. Within months, many of these same regiments would fight desperately at Chancellorsville and Gettysburg. The battle's images‚ÄîUnion dead stacked before the stone wall, wounded freezing through the night‚Äîhaunted both nations.
