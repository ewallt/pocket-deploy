The Moral Law of Power

*Power is safe only when it serves love—and God never departs from this law.*

The rod-and-serpent lesson, the plagues, and the cross converge on one claim: methods matter. The question is not whether God is strong, but how His strength is expressed. Criminal power compels and deceives. Divine power reveals and persuades. The difference is not tactical; it is ontological.

Because God is love, He cannot use anti-love means without denying Himself. He will suffer loss before He coerces victory. That is why wrath in Scripture is the pain of rejected love, not the temper of a volatile sovereign. When protection finally lifts, consequence—not caprice—arrives.

This moral law organizes reality. Where love governs, power builds; where self rules, power destroys. Judgment is the moment this becomes visible to all. It is revelation, not retribution—clarity that heals the teachable and confirms the incorrigible.

The universe is secure precisely because God is not a criminal. He will never “win” by forfeiting His character. The throne is safe for eternity because it is shaped like a cross, and the hands that rule it are scarred with fidelity.

To live under such a King is to use power the same way: truthful, patient, and self-giving. In that imitation, the world learns what God is like.

Sources: Exodus 4–14; Romans 1:18–25; 2 Corinthians 10:1–5; Behold Your God (power that serves love).
