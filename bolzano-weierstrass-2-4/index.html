Series 2 — Bolzano–Weierstrass

Part 4 · The Theorem Itself

We can now state the theorem that bears Bolzano and Weierstrass’s names: every bounded sequence in \(\mathbb{R}\) has a convergent subsequence. More formally, if \((x_n)\) is a sequence and there exists \(M > 0\) with \(|x_n| \le M\) for all \(n\), then there is a subsequence \((x_{n_k})\) and a limit \(L\in\mathbb{R}\) such that \(\lim_{k\to\infty} x_{n_k} = L\). The power of the statement lies in what it promises: even when the original sequence resists convergence, we can extract a convergent thread from its infinite fabric.

The theorem’s hypothesis and conclusion are both sharp. If we drop boundedness, counterexamples abound: \(x_n = n\) has no convergent subsequence. If we weaken the conclusion to “has a Cauchy subsequence,” the result becomes equivalent because in \(\mathbb{R}\) every Cauchy sequence converges. The interplay between boundedness and completeness is precisely what forces convergence; without completeness (as in \(\mathbb{Q}\)), a bounded sequence can have a Cauchy subsequence converging to an irrational number, which lies outside the space.

The standard proof follows the nested-interval method. Begin by enclosing the sequence inside an interval \([a_0,b_0]\) that captures all terms. Bisect the interval into \([a_0, m]\) and \([m, b_0]\) where \(m = (a_0 + b_0)/2\). At least one half contains infinitely many sequence elements; choose that half and label it \([a_1,b_1]\). Repeat the process indefinitely, producing a nested sequence of closed intervals whose lengths shrink by a factor of two each time. Completeness tells us the intersection \(\bigcap [a_k,b_k]\) is nonempty and contains a point \(L\).

To build the subsequence, select indices inductively. Because each interval \([a_k,b_k]\) contains infinitely many terms, we can choose an index \(n_k\) larger than \(n_{k-1}\) with \(x_{n_k} \in [a_k,b_k]\). The resulting subsequence is trapped inside intervals whose diameters approach zero, forcing the terms to converge to \(L\). The ε–N definition confirms this intuition: given any ε, pick \(k\) with \(b_k - a_k < 2\varepsilon\); for all \(j \ge k\), the term \(x_{n_j}\) lies within ε of \(L\).

This proof highlights the hidden infrastructure supporting the theorem. We rely on the completeness of \(\mathbb{R}\) to guarantee a point in the nested intersection and on the Archimedean property to ensure intervals shrink effectively. The strategy also foreshadows compactness arguments: the nested intervals form a decreasing family of nonempty compact sets whose intersection remains nonempty. Recognizing these themes prepares us for the more abstract framing in Series 3.

Before tackling that abstraction, the final article in Series 2 distills proof intuition. We will translate the formal steps into a narrative and explore alternative perspectives—including the monotone subsequence theorem—that lead to the same conclusion.
