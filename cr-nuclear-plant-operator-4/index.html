Lessons from History

*From the Flood to the Cross: patient presence before inevitable collapse.*

The Flood: Divine patience “waited in the days of Noah.” When violence filled the earth, God’s warnings preserved every open door. The deluge reads not as a rage-storm but as unprotected creation returning to chaos once restraining grace was refused.

Sodom: Angels pull Lot out while the city presses in. Abraham’s intercession shows how far God will go to save. Destruction comes only after safety is rejected, revealing a society past hearing, not a deity eager to strike.

Egypt: Plagues escalate in clarity, not cruelty. Each sign educates, times are announced, protections offered. Pharaoh bars the door tighter; the system buckles. Israel walks out under a canopy of mercy while Egypt reaps the harvest of its own hardness.

The Cross: Here is the ultimate lesson. God-with-us remains at the controls under maximum provocation. He refuses to retaliate, then entrusts Himself to the Father. When He finally dies, the world shakes—creation registering the loss of its sustaining Presence.

Across these stories the pattern holds: long-suffering first, withdrawal last; protection offered, protection refused; consequence follows. History, read with the Operator in view, vindicates a government of love.

Sources: 1 Peter 3:20; Genesis 6–7; Genesis 18–19; Exodus 7–12; Matthew 27:45–54; Behold Your God.
