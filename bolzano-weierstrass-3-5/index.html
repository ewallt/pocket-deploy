Series 3 — Machinery & Consequences

Part 5 · Beyond ℝ

Bolzano–Weierstrass scales elegantly to ℝⁿ. Any bounded sequence in Euclidean space has a convergent subsequence. The standard proof applies the one-dimensional theorem to each coordinate, using a diagonal argument. We first extract a subsequence whose first coordinates converge, then refine to ensure the second coordinates converge, and so on. The resulting subsequence converges in ℝⁿ, reinforcing that closed and bounded subsets of ℝⁿ are compact.

In general metric spaces, the theorem survives if the space is sequentially compact or, equivalently in metric settings, compact. Total boundedness plus completeness suffices: every sequence has a Cauchy subsequence, and completeness guarantees convergence. This characterization guides the design of function spaces where Bolzano–Weierstrass-like principles apply.

However, the theorem breaks down in infinite-dimensional normed spaces lacking compactness. Consider the unit ball in \(\ell^2\), the space of square-summable sequences. Although bounded, it is not compact. The sequence of standard basis vectors \(e_n\) is bounded but has no convergent subsequence because the vectors stay a fixed distance apart. This example demonstrates that boundedness without total boundedness permits infinitely many “directions” for the sequence to escape.

Topological vector spaces introduce further subtleties. Weak topologies often restore compactness properties: the Banach–Alaoglu theorem, for example, states that the unit ball in the dual of a normed space is compact in the weak* topology. In such contexts, versions of Bolzano–Weierstrass hold with respect to weaker notions of convergence, producing subsequences that converge weakly even when norm convergence fails.

Beyond metric spaces, sequential compactness can diverge from compactness. In non-first-countable spaces, sequences may not detect all topological phenomena, necessitating nets or filters. Nonetheless, Bolzano–Weierstrass inspires generalizations: every bounded sequence in a compact Hausdorff space has a convergent subnet. These abstractions trace the same theme of extracting order from bounded infinite processes.

Wrapping up Series 3, we see that Bolzano–Weierstrass serves as a gateway to compactness across mathematics. From ℝ to ℝⁿ, to function spaces and dual topologies, the theorem’s spirit persists: boundedness plus completeness-like structure yields convergence. Recognizing where the ingredients succeed or fail equips us to navigate advanced analysis with confidence.
