Series 3 — Machinery & Consequences

Part 4 · EVT & Heine–Cantor

The extreme value theorem (EVT) proclaims that a continuous function on a compact set attains both a maximum and a minimum. On closed intervals, this follows directly from Heine–Borel: the continuous image of a compact set is compact, and compact subsets of ℝ are closed and bounded, hence possess maxima and minima. Bolzano–Weierstrass provides the sequential intuition—every sequence in the domain has a convergent subsequence whose image converges, ensuring that suprema and infima are actually achieved.

The Heine–Cantor theorem delivers uniform continuity on compact sets. If \(f:[a,b]\to ℝ\) is continuous, then for every \(\varepsilon > 0\) there exists \(\delta > 0\) such that whenever \(|x-y| < \delta\), we have \(|f(x) - f(y)| < \varepsilon\). Compactness ensures that the \(\delta\) can be chosen uniformly across the entire interval. The proof typically assumes the contrary and constructs sequences of points whose distances shrink while their images stay apart, contradicting sequential compactness.

These theorems illustrate how compactness transforms pointwise guarantees into global ones. Continuity alone only assures local control; compactness upgrades that to global control by preventing pathological escapes. EVT guarantees extremal values exist, underpinning optimization problems and existence proofs for differential equations. Heine–Cantor supports error estimates in numerical integration and ensures that continuous functions on compact sets are Riemann integrable.

The interplay between EVT and Heine–Cantor highlights the dual roles of subsequences and open covers. EVT often uses sequences: take a maximizing sequence and extract a convergent subsequence. Heine–Cantor can be proved using open covers: for each point, choose a neighborhood where continuity provides a delta, then compactness supplies a finite subcover with a minimum delta. Switching between perspectives strengthens our intuition for compactness-driven arguments.

Beyond ℝ, these results generalize to compact metric spaces. Continuous functions on compact spaces remain bounded and attain their extrema, and uniform continuity follows automatically. However, without compactness, both theorems can fail spectacularly; functions may approach suprema they never reach or display wildly oscillating behavior near boundary points.

With these consequences in mind, we can step outside the real line. The final article examines how Bolzano–Weierstrass extends to higher dimensions and other metric spaces, and where its promises break down.
