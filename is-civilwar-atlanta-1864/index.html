Atlanta (1864)

Sherman's Capture Seals Lincoln's Reelection

Core Summary

After four months of maneuvering and fighting from Chattanooga to Atlanta's outskirts, William T. Sherman captured Atlanta on September 2, 1864. Confederate General John Bell Hood had replaced Joseph Johnston in July, abandoning Johnston's defensive strategy for costly attacks that bled his army white. Hood's three major offensives in ten days‚ÄîPeachtree Creek, Atlanta (Battle of), and Ezra Church‚Äîcost 15,000 irreplaceable casualties. Sherman's repeated flanking movements forced Hood to evacuate Atlanta or be trapped. The city's fall devastated Confederate morale and transformed Northern politics. Lincoln had expected to lose the 1864 election to peace Democrats. Sherman's victory ensured Lincoln's reelection and the war's continuation to unconditional surrender. Sherman telegraphed: "Atlanta is ours, and fairly won."

üéØ Strategic Context

‚ñº
Johnston's Retreat: From May through mid-July, Joseph Johnston conducted a masterful defensive fighting withdrawal from Dalton, Georgia, to Atlanta's doorstep. He preserved his army while frustrating Sherman's 2-to-1 numerical advantage through skillful use of fortified positions and tactical retreats.
Hood Replaces Johnston: Jefferson Davis, demanding offensive action to stop Sherman, replaced Johnston with John Bell Hood on July 17. Hood had a reputation for aggressive combat but lacked Johnston's strategic patience. He promptly attacked Sherman three times in ten days, suffering catastrophic losses.
Sherman's Siege: After Hood's failed attacks bled Confederate strength, Sherman besieged Atlanta while extending cavalry raids to cut rail lines supplying the city. Hood's Tennessee Campaign cavalry raids against Sherman's supply lines failed. With rails cut, Hood evacuated September 1 to avoid encirclement.

ü™ñ Forces & Commanders

‚ñº
Union: Sherman commanded 112,000 troops in three armies: George H. Thomas's Army of the Cumberland (60,000), James McPherson's Army of the Tennessee (25,000), and John Schofield's Army of the Ohio (13,000). Sherman coordinated their movements brilliantly, keeping Confederate forces off-balance.
Confederate: Hood commanded 51,000 troops after assuming command. His aggressive attacks at Peachtree Creek (July 20), Atlanta/Battle of (July 22), and Ezra Church (July 28) cost 15,000 casualties the Confederacy could not replace. By August, Hood's offensive capacity was destroyed.
Casualties: Entire campaign May-September: Union approximately 31,687 total casualties. Confederate approximately 34,979 total casualties. Atlanta's strategic value made the losses worthwhile for the Union; the Confederacy gained nothing but delayed the inevitable.

üìÖ Timeline

‚ñº
May-July 1864: Sherman advances from Chattanooga through northern Georgia, repeatedly flanking Johnston's defensive positions. Johnston retreats skillfully, preserving his army while inflicting casualties. Davis grows impatient with Johnston's Fabian tactics.
July 17: Davis replaces Johnston with Hood, demanding aggressive action to stop Sherman. Hood promises offensive operations to save Atlanta.
July 20: Hood attacks Thomas's Army of the Cumberland at Peachtree Creek as it crosses the stream. The assault fails with 4,800 Confederate casualties versus 1,900 Union. Hood's first offensive accomplishes nothing.
July 22: Hood attacks McPherson's Army of the Tennessee east of Atlanta in the Battle of Atlanta. McPherson is killed‚Äîthe highest-ranking Union officer killed in action during the war. Union forces repulse the attack, inflicting 8,000 Confederate casualties versus 3,700 Union.
July 28: Hood attacks Sherman's right flank at Ezra Church. Another costly failure: 3,000 Confederate casualties versus 600 Union. Hood's three offensives in ten days have bled his army of 15,000 irreplaceable troops.
August: Sherman extends lines westward, cutting railroads supplying Atlanta. Hood's cavalry raids against Sherman's supply lines fail to disrupt Union operations. Sherman's cavalry under Judson Kilpatrick cuts the Macon & Western Railroad‚ÄîAtlanta's last rail connection.
September 1: Hood evacuates Atlanta to avoid encirclement, destroying military supplies and ammunition. Massive explosions rock the city as Confederate ordnance goes up.
September 2: Sherman occupies Atlanta. He telegraphs Washington: "Atlanta is ours, and fairly won." News electrifies the North. Lincoln's reelection is assured.

‚ñº
Johnston's Removal: Davis's decision to replace cautious Johnston with aggressive Hood backfired spectacularly. Hood's three attacks in ten days bled the Confederate army white without stopping Sherman. Johnston might have prolonged the campaign indefinitely; Hood destroyed his army trying to win quickly.
Hood's Failed Offensives: Three major attacks‚ÄîPeachtree Creek, Atlanta, Ezra Church‚Äîcost 15,000 irreplaceable casualties and accomplished nothing. Hood destroyed his army's offensive capability in ten days. By August, he could only defend weakly.
Railroad Destruction: Sherman's cavalry cutting Atlanta's rail lines doomed the city. Hood could not feed his army or the civilian population without rail connections. Evacuation became inevitable once the Macon & Western Railroad was severed.

üìä Outcomes

‚ñº
Lincoln's Reelection: Atlanta's fall transformed the 1864 presidential election. Democrats had nominated George McClellan on a peace platform calling the war a failure. Sherman's victory proved the war could be won, ensuring Lincoln's victory in November with 55% of the popular vote and 212 of 233 electoral votes.
March to the Sea: After capturing Atlanta, Sherman proposed marching to Savannah, cutting a 60-mile-wide swath of destruction through Georgia's heartland. Grant approved. Sherman's March to the Sea brought total war to the Confederate interior, destroying Georgia's capacity to support Confederate armies.
Confederate Collapse Accelerates: Atlanta's loss devastated Southern morale and proved the Confederacy could not protect its vital cities. Hood's disastrous Tennessee Campaign in November-December‚Äîculminating in catastrophic defeat at Nashville‚Äîfinished the Confederacy's western armies.

üîç Historical Significance

‚ñº

Atlanta's capture ranks among the Civil War's most significant events. Historian James McPherson argues it "ensured Lincoln's reelection and thus the war's continuation to unconditional Union victory and slave emancipation." Without Atlanta, McClellan might have won the presidency on a platform of negotiated peace that could have preserved slavery and Confederate independence. Sherman's campaign demonstrated his operational brilliance. Unlike Grant's costly frontal assaults in Virginia, Sherman maneuvered Hood out of position after position. When Hood replaced Johnston and launched desperate attacks, Sherman bloodied Confederate forces in defensive battles. Hood's replacement of Johnston proved one of the war's great Confederate blunders‚Äîhis attacks accomplished nothing but casualties his army could not afford. Atlanta's fall opened Georgia's interior to Sherman's March to the Sea, bringing total war to the Confederate heartland. Civilians experienced war's destructive power firsthand as Sherman's troops lived off the land and destroyed everything of military value. Sherman's famous dictum‚Äî"War is hell"‚Äîreflected his understanding that breaking Southern will required destroying their capacity and will to wage war. Atlanta proved Sherman right.
