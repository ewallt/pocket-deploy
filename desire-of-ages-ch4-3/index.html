Unto You a Saviour — Part 3: Joy for All People

Bethlehem’s announcement refuses exclusivity: heaven’s joy is intentionally sized for every tribe, station, and story.

The angel’s phrasing matters: “good tidings of great joy … to all people.” The gospel begins by knocking down the walls we habitually build—between holy and common, clean and unclean, center and margins. The first hearers are night-shift laborers without rank or reputation, which is itself commentary. Joy lands where pride would never send it, then radiates outward. This is not vague optimism; it is a concrete promise attached to a concrete child whose presence will be discoverable by anyone willing to stoop. If the sign is small enough for shepherds, it is small enough for us.

“All people” signals a widening arc that will sweep beyond Israel without erasing Israel. The child born in David’s city fulfills covenant hopes while overturning narrow readings of privilege. First the poor, then the forgotten, then the foreigner will find themselves named in this news. A manger in Judea will eventually make room for Greeks, Romans, Ethiopians, and the far horizons of our neighborhoods. The chapter trains our instincts: expect grace to run downhill, pooling in low places; expect the Spirit to highlight the faces the powerful overlook; expect joy to feel like welcome.

Joy here is moral, not merely emotional. It is what happens when the world receives a truer knowledge of God and begins to breathe again. Fear loosens its grip; shame loses its script; cynicism finds itself contradicted by kindness in motion. Such joy is demanding because it invites repentance—the surrender of our preferred pecking orders and the relinquishment of contempt. It is also durable because it rests on Reality Himself. The Child ensures that gladness is not escapism but alignment with the grain of a universe governed by self-giving love.

Communal joy becomes visible in practices: shared food, open doors, songs that ordinary voices can sing. The shepherds are not handed a manifesto; they are given directions, a doxology, and a baby to behold. Communities formed by this chapter will learn to celebrate small mercies without embarrassment, to tell the truth about sorrow without losing their song, and to make space for strangers without counting the cost first. In such places, testimony is not curated performance but grateful overflow: “We went, we saw, and we cannot be quiet.”

There is also a stubborn edge to this joy. It resists imperial narratives that equate peace with dominance or prosperity with divine preference. It refuses to be weaponized by ideology or narrowed by nostalgia. It insists that the smile of God is over the whole earth, with special tenderness toward the bruised and the baffled. It leans toward reconciliation, redistribution, repair. In this sense, “for all people” is both comfort and commission: comfort because none are excluded; commission because we must learn to live accordingly.

Practically, the path is simple enough to attempt today. We rehearse the angel’s sentence over the places we inhabit—homes, offices, classrooms, streets—and ask for the courage to embody it. We allow our calendars to include persons who cannot repay us. We watch for signs sized to human hands and respond before the moment passes. In doing so, we discover that joy is not scarce. It multiplies as it moves, proving the messenger right and the manger sufficient for the world God so loves.

Source: Ellen G. White, The Desire of Ages, Chapter 4 “Unto You a Saviour”.
