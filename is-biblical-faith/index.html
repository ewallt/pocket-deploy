Robert J. Wieland: A Voice Calling the Church Back to 1888

How a missionary's encounter with God's relentless grace led him to spend his life calling for corporate repentance and a return to the gospel of the cross.

Seventh-day Adventist missionary & evangelist (1916‚Äì2011)
Co-author of 1888 Re-Examined
Key themes: righteousness by faith, corporate repentance, Christ's faithfulness

Robert Wieland was born in 1916 and grew up in a devout Seventh-day Adventist home. As a young man he felt called to missionary service and spent years in Africa, where he experienced both the rigors of evangelism and the deep joy of seeing people encounter Christ. But it was not on the mission field that Wieland's theology took its defining turn‚Äîit was in his study of Adventist history, especially the story of what happened at the 1888 General Conference session in Minneapolis.

In 1888, two young Adventist preachers, Ellet J. Waggoner and Alonzo T. Jones, brought a message to the church that emphasized justification by faith in Christ alone, the centrality of the cross, and the love of God as the motive for obedience. Ellen White endorsed the message as "most precious," declaring it to be heaven-sent light. Yet the church's leadership largely resisted, and the message was effectively shelved. Wieland became convinced that this rejection had shaped Adventism for generations, leaving it with a gospel weakened by legalism and fear.

In the 1950s, Wieland and his colleague Donald K. Short wrote 1888 Re-Examined, a book that argued the Adventist church had corporately rejected a divine revelation and that it needed to corporately repent. The book was controversial‚Äîsome saw it as a prophetic call to renewal, others as divisive criticism. Wieland endured decades of tension with church leadership, but he never backed down from his central conviction: that the gospel is God's initiative in Christ, not human performance, and that faith is trusting reception of already-given grace.

Faith as Beholding, Not Striving

Wieland's writings return again and again to the idea that biblical faith is not willpower or determination but heart-appreciation kindled by beholding the love of God. He taught that the cross reveals the Father's character, and that when the heart truly sees that revelation, repentance and obedience flow naturally. Fear-based religion, he insisted, produces only superficial conformity; love awakens genuine transformation. For Wieland, the issue was never whether God was pleased with us, but whether we had seen how pleased God already is to give us His Son.

This led him to reframe traditional Adventist teachings on the investigative judgment and the law. He argued that judgment is not God looking for reasons to condemn but God publicly vindicating His grace and the transformed character of His people. The law's purpose is not to terrify but to reveal the beauty of God's character and invite us into relationship. And at the center of everything stands Christ's faithfulness‚Äînot ours‚Äîas the anchor of assurance. Wieland loved to emphasize Galatians 2:16 and the phrase "the faith of Jesus," insisting that it is Christ's own faithful obedience that saves us, and our faith is simply receiving that gift.

How this page reflects Wieland's influence
  • The emphasis on faith as heart-appreciation awakened by beholding God's goodness mirrors Wieland's lifelong teaching.
  • The refusal to treat faith as human achievement or willpower echoes his critique of legalistic religion.
  • The focus on Christ's faithfulness as the anchor, not our own performance, reflects his reading of Galatians 2:16.
  • The call for love rather than fear as the motive for obedience is central to his vision of the 1888 message.

To speak here of heart-trust awakened by God's love, of faith as gift rather than achievement, and of Christ's faithfulness as our anchor is to walk the path Wieland marked out: a return to the most precious message of righteousness by faith that he believed the church still needed to fully receive.
