Calvary — Part 3: “With Me in Paradise”

Between two criminals, the Crucified opens a door: repentance recognized, royalty confessed, hope spoken into pain.

Two men hang beside Jesus, close enough to read the title and hear the prayers. One joins the chorus of scorn, demanding rescue without repentance. The other begins to see. He rebukes his partner, confesses justice in his own sentence, and recognizes innocence in the Man at the center. Then he turns with a request shaped like faith: “Lord, remember me when You come into Your kingdom.” He asks not for release from wood but for mercy from a King whose crown is love.

Jesus answers immediately and personally. He does not survey history or recite a creed; He gives a promise that carries the weight of His name. The assurance is relational—“with Me”—because paradise is not primarily geography but communion restored. It is also royal: the One mocked as pretender speaks like a sovereign who can keep what He says. In a scene designed to humiliate, a kingdom quietly advances by adopting a dying thief as its first public trophy.

The exchange reveals the nature of saving faith. It is not fluency in theology but truth told about self and truth trusted about Jesus. The penitent thief sees enough to stake everything—his guilt, Christ’s goodness, and a future only God could grant. He cannot move hands or feet, but he moves his heart toward the Light. Grace travels faster than nails can hold, and the cross becomes a doorway before it becomes a tomb’s key.

It also reveals the King’s heart under pressure. In agony, Jesus still hears a whisper from the margins and answers without irritation. He is not too preoccupied with cosmic victory to notice a single plea. The government He embodies does not triage the undeserving; it delights to give where nothing can be repaid. Even here, He practices what He preached: the lost found, the last welcomed, the sinner remembered.

For us, the thief’s prayer becomes a pattern. We bring the truth we cannot spin and ask to be remembered by the One whose memory saves. We do not bargain with records; we appeal to relationship. And we learn to speak of future hope in the worst of present rooms, because the promise rests on a Person who cannot lie and will not lose those the Father has given Him.

Calvary thus sings a quieter verse beneath the taunts. A dying man calls a dying King “Lord,” and the King answers like morning. By the time the crowd goes home, one criminal’s story has been rewritten. So will ours, if we ask for the same remembrance and trust the same royal mercy that turned a cross into a pledge.

Source: Ellen G. White, The Desire of Ages, Chapter 78 “Calvary”.
