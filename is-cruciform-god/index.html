The Cruciform God: The Cross as the Full Revelation of God

Power Redefined as Self-Giving, Enemy-Loving Love

Core Thesis

The decisive revelation of who God is‚Äîand of how God rules‚Äîis the cross of Jesus Christ. In the crucified Messiah, divine power appears as self-giving, non-coercive love that defeats evil by truth and sacrificial faithfulness rather than force (John 12:31-33; 1 Corinthians 1:18-25; Colossians 2:15). All Scripture and all conceptions of God are to be interpreted through this cruciform center (Hebrews 1:1-3; John 14:9). Atonement, judgment, and victory are therefore restorative and revelatory: God reconciles the world to Himself by unveiling His faithful love and drawing hearts into trusting allegiance (2 Corinthians 5:19-21; Colossians 1:19-20).

Influential Voice

Gregory A. Boyd: From Certainty to the Crucified God

How a crisis of faith led a pastor and scholar to rediscover the cross as the lens through which to read every difficult text and doctrine.

American evangelical theologian (b. 1957)
Author of The Crucifixion of the Warrior God & Cross Vision
Key themes: cruciform hermeneutic, Christus Victor, open theism, cosmic conflict

Greg Boyd grew up in a non-religious home and became an atheist in his teenage years. But in college he had a dramatic conversion experience and plunged into evangelical Christianity with the zeal of a new believer. He went on to earn a PhD in theology and eventually became the senior pastor of Woodland Hills Church in Minnesota, where he preached and taught with confidence about God's sovereignty, biblical authority, and the clarity of Christian doctrine.

But as the years went on, Boyd found himself increasingly troubled by the violent portraits of God in the Old Testament‚Äîthe commands to slaughter entire cities, the plagues, the wrath. These texts seemed impossible to reconcile with the Jesus who blessed children, healed lepers, forgave enemies, and died on a Roman cross without retaliation. For a time, Boyd tried the standard apologetic moves: God's wrath is just, God's ways are higher than ours, the Canaanites were deeply wicked. But none of it satisfied him. The tension gnawed at him until it became a crisis of faith.

The breakthrough came when Boyd began to take seriously the claim that Jesus is the full and final revelation of God‚Äînot one revelation among many, but the decisive one that redefines everything else. If "whoever has seen me has seen the Father," then the cross must be the lens through which we read all of Scripture, including the difficult texts. Boyd developed what he calls a "cruciform hermeneutic": the conviction that God's true character is most clearly revealed in the self-giving, non-retaliatory love of the crucified Christ, and that earlier, more violent depictions of God in Scripture represent God "bearing" or "accommodating" human sin and misunderstanding in order to meet people where they are and lead them toward the truth fully revealed in Jesus.

The Cross as the Definitive Portrait

Boyd's major work, The Crucifixion of the Warrior God, is a massive two-volume argument that the cross must control how we interpret every text and doctrine. He insists that God's power is not coercive force but persuasive, self-giving love. The crucified Christ unmasks the powers of accusation and violence, disarming them by absorbing their fury without retaliation. This is not weakness‚Äîit is the truest strength, the power that wins hearts and defeats evil by truth rather than domination.

This cruciform reading led Boyd to champion what he calls the "Christus Victor" model of atonement, in which the cross is primarily about God defeating the powers of sin, death, and the devil through suffering love. It also led him into "open theism," the view that God genuinely risks and relates in freedom with creation, and into a renewed emphasis on the cosmic conflict theme‚Äîthe idea that much of the violence and evil in the world (and even in Scripture's depiction of God) reflects spiritual warfare rather than God's direct action.

How this page reflects Boyd's influence
  • The call to read all Scripture through the cruciform center is Boyd's signature move.
  • The emphasis on power as persuasion, not coercion echoes his critique of classical theism's view of sovereignty.
  • The language of sin-bearing accommodation‚ÄîGod meeting us in our fallen lenses‚Äîcomes directly from his work.
  • The focus on Christus Victor and the defeat of accusatory powers mirrors his atonement theology.

To insist here that the cross is the full revelation of God's character, that power is redefined as cruciform love, and that all difficult texts must be read through Jesus is to walk the path Greg Boyd carved out in his own journey from certainty through crisis to a deeper, cross-centered faith.
