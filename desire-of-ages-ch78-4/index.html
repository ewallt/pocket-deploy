Calvary — Part 4: “Behold Your Mother”

At the cross, Jesus honors the fifth commandment and forms a family that will outlive death.

Near the wood stand women who do not leave. Mary is there, and beside her the disciple Jesus loves. Between seizures of pain, the Son sees the mother who once carried Him and the friend who will carry His gospel. He speaks not as victim but as eldest Son keeping covenant: “Woman, behold your son.” Then to John, “Behold your mother.” From that hour, the disciple takes her to his own home. Duty becomes devotion under the shadow of a cross.

This word protects tenderness from being swallowed by mission. Salvation of the world does not bypass particular loves; it deepens them. The One who bears every burden still remembers a widow’s future, a household’s provision, a mother’s ache. He refuses the false nobility that sacrifices the small on the altar of the large. In His kingdom, obedience to God never cancels care for family; it fulfills it with larger resources.

Mary’s path is honored here. She once stored angelic promises and shepherds’ songs; now she stores a sword. The prophecy of Simeon passes through her as her Son suffers within reach yet beyond rescue. Jesus does not hide her from the cost of His calling, but He refuses to leave her without companionship. The church learns from this moment that suffering saints need both theology and kitchens, both hope and homes.

John’s role is likewise instructive. He is not chosen for pedigree but for proximity and love. The entrusted “mother” becomes a sacrament of discipleship: to receive those Jesus loves as our own, to reorder our lives around their care, to welcome responsibility as privilege. Pastors, friends, and communities find their templates here—a cross-formed hospitality that refuses to let anyone bleed alone.

At a deeper level, the word creates a new family under the cross. Water and Spirit have always made kin; now suffering seals it. Believers will call one another brothers and sisters not as metaphor but as mission. We will steward widows, welcome orphans, and make homes porous to need. The commandment to honor father and mother expands as the church recognizes parents and children given by grace.

Thus Calvary’s tenderness interprets Calvary’s power. The Lord who forgives enemies also arranges groceries. The Saviour who promises paradise also provides a place to sleep. Holiness is not aloof; it is attentive. If we wish to stand near the cross, we will learn to love this concretely—forming households of faith where those Jesus entrusts to us are safe, seen, and sustained.

Source: Ellen G. White, The Desire of Ages, Chapter 78 “Calvary”.
