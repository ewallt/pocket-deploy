Open Theism: Criticisms, Implications, and Contemporary Relevance

Part 13 · Practical Implications: Prayer, Trust, and Living with Uncertainty

Theological debates can often seem abstract, but the differences between Open Theism and classical views have significant practical implications for a believer's daily life, particularly concerning prayer, trust, and dealing with suffering.

Prayer: In Open Theism, prayer is understood as a genuinely influential activity. It is a partnership with God to shape the future. When a person prays, they are not simply aligning their will with a predetermined divine plan or asking God to do what He was already going to do. Instead, petitionary prayer can actually change what happens. God responds to our prayers and may alter His plans accordingly. This makes prayer an act of immense responsibility and significance. The future is not yet written, and our prayers can help write it. This view, proponents say, motivates fervent, persistent prayer in a way that deterministic models cannot.

Trust and Guidance: How does one trust a God who doesn't have every detail of the future mapped out? Open Theists argue that trust is placed in the Person of God, not in a predetermined plan. We trust in God's perfect character—His love, wisdom, and faithfulness. We trust His ability to respond to any situation with infinite resourcefulness and to work toward His ultimate goals, even through unexpected turns. Divine guidance is not about discovering a pre-written script for our lives, but about walking in a close relationship with God, making wise choices based on His revealed will and character, and trusting Him to guide and correct us along the way.

Suffering and Evil: Open Theism offers a distinct perspective on the problem of evil. Since the future is open and God has granted genuine freedom, much of the evil in the world is the direct result of the misuse of that freedom by created beings (human and spiritual). God is not the author or permitter of evil in the sense that it is part of His eternal decree. Rather, He genuinely grieves over sin and suffering and actively works to combat it. He is not a distant stage manager but a fellow sufferer who enters into our pain and works to bring redemption out of it. This can provide comfort to those who struggle with the idea of a God who meticulously planned a world containing immense suffering.

Living with Uncertainty: An open future inherently involves a degree of uncertainty. We cannot be sure what the future holds, and neither, in some respects, can God. Critics see this as a source of anxiety. Proponents, however, see it as the necessary context for genuine faith, hope, and love. Faith is not about certainty in a detailed blueprint, but about radical trust in a good and wise God. Hope is not the passive expectation of an inevitable outcome, but the active anticipation of a future we are co-creating with God. And love is not pre-programmed, but a free and meaningful choice made in the face of an open horizon. Living in this "adventure" with God, they argue, is what makes life truly meaningful.
