The Problem with Servant Religion

Obedience born of fear cannot mature into friendship.

Servant religion defines faith as submission without understanding. It demands performance, measures compliance, and treats God as an exacting master. Maxwell exposes how this approach contradicts the gospel. Love cannot be commanded by fear; friendship cannot survive coercion.

When people worship God to avoid punishment, they obey for self-preservation, not trust. The result is the same spirit that corrupted heaven — service without love. True worship arises from admiration of God’s goodness, not anxiety about His power.

Jesus’ parables show the difference. The prodigal’s elder brother obeyed but resented his father; the faithful servant feared the master’s return. Both missed the joy of relationship. God’s goal is not obedience alone but intelligent loyalty.

Servant religion also distorts God’s character. If He must threaten to secure obedience, He becomes indistinguishable from the tyrants He condemns. The cross refutes that idea: God wins hearts by revealing love, not by enforcing submission.

Only when fear is gone can obedience become the natural expression of friendship — the spontaneous cooperation of love with Love.

Sources: *Servants or Friends?*, Ch. 3 “The Problem with Servant Religion.”
