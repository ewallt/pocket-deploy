Cantor and the Infinite: Revolutionizing Numbers

Part 4 · Countable Infinity: The Size of Natural Numbers (ℵ₀)

Cantor defined a set as countably infinite if it can be put into one-to-one correspondence with the natural numbers ℕ = {0, 1, 2, 3, ...}. The cardinality of ℕ is denoted ℵ₀ (aleph-null), the smallest transfinite cardinal number. A set is countable if it is either finite or countably infinite. Countably infinite sets are, in a precise sense, the "smallest" kind of infinity. Despite being infinite, they can be "counted" or "listed" in a sequence, indexed by natural numbers. This notion of countability became central to Cantor's theory, distinguishing sets that, though infinite, share the fundamental structure of ℕ from those that do not.

The integers ℤ = {..., −2, −1, 0, 1, 2, ...} are countably infinite, even though they extend infinitely in both directions. To see this, construct a bijection with ℕ: pair 0 ↔ 0, 1 ↔ 1, −1 ↔ 2, 2 ↔ 3, −2 ↔ 4, 3 ↔ 5, and so on. This "zigzag" listing alternates between positive and negative integers, ensuring every integer appears exactly once. The function f: ℕ → ℤ defined by f(n) = n/2 if n is even, and f(n) = −(n+1)/2 if n is odd, is a bijection. Thus, |ℤ| = |ℕ| = ℵ₀. Despite having twice as many "directions" (positive and negative), the integers have the same cardinality as the natural numbers, a counterintuitive but rigorous result.

More surprisingly, the rational numbers ℚ are also countably infinite. The rationals seem far more numerous than the integers: between any two rational numbers lie infinitely many others, forming a dense set on the number line. Yet Cantor proved that ℚ can be listed in a sequence, demonstrating |ℚ| = ℵ₀. His argument uses a systematic enumeration. Represent each rational number p/q (in lowest terms, q > 0) as a pair (p, q). Arrange these pairs in a grid, with p along one axis and q along the other. Then traverse the grid diagonally: (0,1), (1,1), (−1,1), (0,2), (1,2), (2,1), (−2,1), ... skipping duplicates (e.g., 2/4 is skipped since it equals 1/2). This diagonal enumeration lists every rational exactly once, constructing a bijection with ℕ.

Cantor's diagonal method for enumerating ℚ is ingenious. Instead of trying to list rationals in numerical order (which cannot be done, as there is no smallest positive rational), he exploits the two-dimensional structure of rational numbers as pairs (numerator, denominator). By systematically covering all pairs, ordered by the sum |p| + q, he ensures completeness. This technique—reducing a two-dimensional problem to a one-dimensional sequence—illustrates a key principle in dealing with countable sets: multiple infinite dimensions can sometimes be "flattened" into a single countable sequence. The rationals, despite their density and apparent profusion, have the same cardinality as the sparse natural numbers.

Cantor also proved that the union of countably many countable sets is countable. If A₁, A₂, A₃, ... are each countable, then their union ⋃ᵢ Aᵢ is countable. To see this, list each Aᵢ as a sequence: A₁ = {a₁₁, a₁₂, a₁₃, ...}, A₂ = {a₂₁, a₂₂, a₂₃, ...}, and so on. Arrange these elements in a two-dimensional array, then enumerate diagonally, as with the rationals. This ensures every element of every Aᵢ appears in the combined sequence. Applications include showing that the set of all finite sequences of natural numbers, or the set of algebraic numbers (solutions to polynomial equations with integer coefficients), are countable. Algebraic numbers include all rationals and many irrationals (like √2, ∛3), yet they remain countable—a surprising result given their ubiquity in algebra.

The Cartesian product ℕ × ℕ of natural numbers with itself is countable, proven by the same diagonal argument used for ℚ. Pairs (m, n) can be listed systematically: (0,0), (0,1), (1,0), (0,2), (1,1), (2,0), ... ordered by m + n. This extends to finite Cartesian products: ℕ × ℕ × ··· × ℕ (k times) is countable for any fixed k. However, infinite products behave differently. The set ℕ^ℕ of all infinite sequences of natural numbers is uncountable, as Cantor later proved using a variant of his diagonal argument. This marks a boundary: finite combinations of countable sets remain countable, but infinite combinations can leap to larger cardinalities.

Countable infinity, characterized by bijection with ℕ and denoted ℵ₀, represents the baseline of infinity in Cantor's hierarchy. Integers, rationals, algebraic numbers, and many other familiar infinite sets all share this cardinality. Despite their differences—density, algebraic structure, geometric distribution—they are, from the perspective of cardinality, equivalent. This unification under ℵ₀ reveals a deep structural commonality among disparate mathematical objects. Yet ℵ₀ is not the only infinity. Cantor's next revelation—that the real numbers ℝ are uncountable—would demonstrate that infinities come in fundamentally different sizes. Countable infinity, though infinite, is merely the first step in an endless hierarchy of transfinite cardinals, each larger than the last, extending far beyond human intuition into the abstract realm of pure mathematics.
