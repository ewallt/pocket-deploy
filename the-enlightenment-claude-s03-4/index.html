The Enlightenment: Revolutions, Critiques, and Legacy

Part 4 · Romanticism and Counter-Enlightenment: Critiques of Pure Reason

Romanticism emerged in the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries as a cultural movement emphasizing emotion, imagination, nature, and individual expression against what Romantics perceived as Enlightenment's cold rationalism and mechanistic worldview. Romantic poets, artists, and philosophers celebrated subjective experience, spontaneous feeling, and creative genius over systematic analysis and universal rules. They found truth and meaning in emotional intuition, aesthetic experience, and connection with nature rather than in abstract reason alone. William Wordsworth, Samuel Taylor Coleridge, Johann Wolfgang von Goethe, and Friedrich Schiller represented literary Romanticism's rejection of Enlightenment rationalism's supposed emotional sterility. This cultural shift reflected disillusionment with Enlightenment optimism following revolutionary violence and disappointment with industrial modernity's alienating effects.

Romantic philosophers and poets valorized emotion and intuition as sources of deeper truth inaccessible to analytical reason. Wordsworth's poetry celebrated childhood innocence, rustic life, and nature's spiritual significance against urban artificiality and scientific reductionism. The Romantic image of the child or noble savage represented uncorrupted humanity before society's distorting influences—themes resonating with Rousseau's critique of civilization, though extending his insights in more emotional and less rationalist directions. Romantics argued that scientific analysis dissects and kills its object, while sympathetic imagination grasps living wholes. They preferred organic metaphors of growth and development to mechanical images of clockwork universe. Emotional authenticity and intuitive insight took precedence over logical consistency and empirical verification as paths to meaningful understanding.

The Counter-Enlightenment, a term coined by Isaiah Berlin, refers to diverse thinkers who rejected core Enlightenment commitments to universal reason, progress, and cosmopolitan values. Johann Georg Hamann and Johann Gottfried Herder criticized Enlightenment rationalism for neglecting cultural particularity, historical context, and linguistic diversity. Herder emphasized that each culture (Volk) develops unique traditions, languages, and values reflecting its historical experience and environment. Universal reason cannot grasp or judge these particularistic forms of life from outside. Herder's cultural nationalism influenced Romantic emphasis on folk traditions, national languages, and historical rootedness against Enlightenment cosmopolitanism. Joseph de Maistre represented reactionary Counter-Enlightenment, defending throne and altar, attacking rationalist hubris, and celebrating tradition, hierarchy, and revealed religion against philosophe secularism and egalitarianism.

Romanticism and Counter-Enlightenment challenged Enlightenment faith in progress and human perfectibility. Burke's conservatism, responding to French Revolution, warned that abstract rationalism destroys valuable traditions embodying accumulated wisdom. Friedrich Nietzsche, though later, extended Romantic critique, attacking Enlightenment's rationalist morality and optimistic anthropology. He argued that reason serves deeper instincts and will to power rather than revealing objective truths. Romanticism often embraced historical pessimism or cyclical views over linear progress. This skepticism reflected recognition of human irrationality, persistent conflict, and tragic dimension of existence that Enlightenment optimism seemed to ignore. The Romantic and Counter-Enlightenment thinkers exposed limits of reason and fragility of civilized order against Enlightenment confidence in rational improvement.

Nature held different meanings for Romantics than for Enlightenment philosophes. Where Newton and his followers saw nature as mechanical system governed by mathematical laws, Romantics experienced nature as living, spiritual presence evoking awe and transcendence. The sublime in nature—storms, mountains, oceans—inspired feelings of terror and wonder revealing human finitude and nature's overwhelming power. Romantic poets like Wordsworth found spiritual renewal and moral instruction in natural landscapes. Pantheist and panentheist views, identifying God with nature or seeing divine presence throughout nature, replaced deist clockmaker deity. This Romantic nature mysticism influenced environmental thought and critiques of instrumental rationality treating nature merely as resource for human exploitation. Where Enlightenment emphasized mastering nature through science, Romanticism stressed communing with nature through aesthetic and spiritual experience.

German Idealism, developed by Fichte, Schelling, and Hegel, synthesized Enlightenment and Romantic elements while transcending both. These philosophers built on Kant's critical philosophy but rejected his limitation of knowledge to phenomena, insisting that thought could grasp ultimate reality. Hegel's system portrayed history as rational process unfolding toward freedom and self-consciousness, preserving Enlightenment's progressive vision while incorporating Romantic emphases on historical development, cultural particularity, and concrete totality over abstract universality. Idealism influenced nineteenth-century thought profoundly, though it too faced criticism for rationalist overreach and speculative excess. The Idealist synthesis represented one response to tensions between Enlightenment reason and Romantic feeling, seeking to integrate both within comprehensive philosophical systems.

The Romantic and Counter-Enlightenment legacy includes both valuable corrections to Enlightenment limitations and dangerous irrationalism. Romantics rightly emphasized emotion, imagination, and aesthetic experience as important dimensions of human life neglected by narrowly rationalist approaches. Their attention to cultural diversity, historical particularity, and limits of universal reason enriched subsequent thought. However, Romantic celebration of emotion over reason, particular over universal, and tradition over critical inquiry also enabled nationalism, obscurantism, and reactionary politics. Counter-Enlightenment skepticism about reason and progress could justify existing injustices and resist beneficial reform. The dialogue between Enlightenment and its critics remains productive: neither pure rationalism nor pure Romanticism adequately captures human experience, which requires both critical reason and meaningful feeling, both universal principles and cultural particularity. Modern thought continues negotiating this tension, suggesting that Enlightenment and Romanticism represent complementary rather than contradictory perspectives on human existence.
