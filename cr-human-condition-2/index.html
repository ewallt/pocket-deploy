The Damage of Fear

*False views of God wound the soul and breed mistrust everywhere else.*

After the fall, Adam hides not because God has changed but because his perception has. Fear replaces friendship, and hiding replaces conversation. That pattern repeats in every generation: when we believe love is conditional, we protect ourselves from it, even while craving it.

Fear distorts relationships. It turns neighbors into rivals, authority into threat, and even prayer into performance. The anxious heart projects its own unease onto heaven, imagining divine judgment behind every shadow. The result is religion without rest and morality without joy.

Scripture counters this paralysis by revealing perfect love that casts out fear. The prophets show God pursuing the frightened, not punishing them for their trembling. Jesus embodies this pursuit when He touches the untouchable and welcomes those convinced they are unwelcome.

Fear cannot be expelled by argument alone; it yields to experience. Trust grows when mercy is encountered in places where punishment was expected. Grace surprises the frightened conscience into hope.

Healing begins when we allow ourselves to be seen without disguise. The same Presence that asked, “Where are you?” still asks—not to expose but to restore. The voice that once walked in Eden now walks in the wounded heart, calling it home.

Sources: Genesis 3:9–10; 1 John 4:18; Luke 5:12–13; Behold Your God (fear and distorted views of God).
