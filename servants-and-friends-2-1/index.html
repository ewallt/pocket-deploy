The Violent God Question

How can a God of love appear so harsh in parts of the Bible?

Maxwell begins this section by addressing the question most likely to distort our view of God: the violence in Scripture. He insists that the problem lies not in God’s character but in our reading of events through fear and projection. The same God revealed in Jesus was the One who dealt with Egypt, Canaan, and Israel — but always in mercy restrained by purpose.

When we see divine actions apart from context, they appear cruel. In reality, every severe act was a reluctant concession to preserve life or truth in a collapsing world. God’s justice is never retribution but containment of evil. He disciplines to heal, not to destroy.

Jesus settled the question of God’s character forever. The cross, not the conquest, is the ultimate revelation of how God deals with sin. Any interpretation that contradicts Calvary must be reconsidered.

Thus, the so-called “violent” portraits are windows into a God who bears consequences rather than inflicts them. Love that suffers is the thread that ties both Testaments together.

Seen through Christ, every story becomes part of the same purpose: to reveal that the Almighty would rather be misunderstood than unloving.

Sources: *Servants or Friends?*, Ch. 6 “The Violent God Question.”
