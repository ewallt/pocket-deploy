The True Operator

*He stays at the controls until he is physically forced out—and then weeps as consequence unfolds.*

Retell the parable. Same plant, same hostility, same danger. Only this time the operator does not abandon his post. He endures insults, stones, threats. He keeps the systems stable, averting failure day after day. When the mob finally bars the door and drives him out, meltdown is no longer preventable. The catastrophe is inevitable, not inflicted.

This is the gospel-shaped picture of divine judgment. God’s presence is the world’s life-support. He suffers long, absorbs abuse, and maintains what sinners cannot. Only when expelled does He withdraw, and even then with tears. The result is not payback but the natural end of unprotected corruption.

To say “God destroyed” is therefore to speak covenant shorthand. It names the event from above—what followed His rejected protection—without confusing motive or method. The moral cause is our insistence on absence, not His resolve to retaliate.

In this light, “wrath” becomes intelligible. It is love refusing to sanction a lie about safety apart from God. His patience delays collapse; His departure merely unveils it. The Cross, where God endures our violence rather than return it, proves that heaven never destroys by anger.

The true Operator reframes every hard story. If destruction follows, it is because we slammed the door on the only Presence that could hold the system together.

Sources: Behold Your God, “God Does Destroy: But How?” (nuclear operator, true telling).
