Open Theism: Scriptural Basis, History, and Key Debates

Part 9 · Historical Roots and Precedents: Is Open Theism New?

A common criticism of Open Theism is that it is a recent theological innovation, a departure from two millennia of orthodox Christian belief. Critics often argue that the overwhelming consensus of church history has been in favor of God's exhaustive, definite foreknowledge. While Open Theism as a formally articulated system is indeed recent—emerging primarily in the late 20th century—proponents argue that its core ideas have precedents throughout church history.

In the early church (2nd-3rd centuries), many theologians, known as the Church Fathers, wrote in a context heavily influenced by Greek ideas of fate and determinism. In response, they strongly emphasized human freedom and moral responsibility. Figures like Justin Martyr and Irenaeus stressed that humans have the power of free choice, a gift from God that makes them accountable for their actions. While they did not develop a full-fledged doctrine of God's knowledge, their defense of libertarian freedom laid a groundwork that is highly compatible with the Open view.

However, the most significant shift occurred with the work of Augustine (4th-5th century), whose theological system strongly affirmed God's meticulous providence and exhaustive foreknowledge. Augustine's influence was immense, and his views became foundational for much of Western theology, both Catholic and Protestant. The classical view of God's omniscience, timelessness, and immutability became the dominant, orthodox position.

Despite this, dissenting voices appeared. In the 16th century, the Arminian movement, led by Jacobus Arminius, challenged the strict determinism of Calvinism. While Arminians affirm God's exhaustive foreknowledge, their strong emphasis on free will created a theological tension that Open Theism later sought to resolve. Arminianism holds that God foreknows free choices, while Open Theism holds that such a concept is incoherent.

A more direct precedent can be found in the Socinians, a radical Reformation movement in the 16th and 17th centuries. Thinkers like Faustus Socinus explicitly argued that God's omniscience did not include knowledge of future contingent events, as such knowledge was impossible by definition. However, the Socinians were also anti-Trinitarian and were widely condemned as heretics, so their theological influence was limited.

The modern formulation of Open Theism emerged in the late 20th century, largely within evangelical circles. Theologians and philosophers like Clark Pinnock, Richard Rice, John Sanders, Gregory Boyd, and William Hasker began to systematically argue that the classical view of God was overly indebted to Greek philosophy and that a more biblical and coherent model was one of a God who takes risks and interacts with genuinely free creatures. So, while the label and the systematic arguments are new, Open Theists maintain that they are recovering a biblical and relational understanding of God that has been present, though often suppressed, throughout the church's history.
