Wrath as Love’s Sorrow

God’s wrath is not revenge but the anguish of rejected love.

Maxwell dismantles the myth of a split personality in God. He writes that wrath is not an explosion of temper but the tragic withdrawal of protection when love is refused. It is love suffering the consequences of being unwanted.

The Old Testament portrays this repeatedly: God “gave them up.” Paul uses the same phrase in Romans 1. Wrath is permissive, not punitive — letting people reap what they insist on sowing.

The cross shows this perfectly. Christ bore the results of human rejection, not divine retaliation. He felt the silence of separation that sin creates, and it broke His heart.

To call this wrath is to speak of love in pain. It is the grief of a parent watching a child self-destruct, not the fury of a ruler enforcing order.

Such understanding transforms our picture of judgment. The fire is not aimed at sinners but burns in every heart that resists love. It consumes only what cannot live with mercy.

Wrath, in Maxwell’s view, is love’s final honesty — the unveiled reality of what happens when the Source of life is pushed away.
