Graham Maxwell: Can God Be Trusted?

How a professor's question-and-answer style and relentless focus on God's trustworthiness reshaped Adventist theology for a generation.

Seventh-day Adventist theologian & teacher (1921‚Äì2010)
Author of Can God Be Trusted? & Servants or Friends?
Key themes: God's character, great controversy as trust issue, government by persuasion

Graham Maxwell was born in 1921 and grew up in a Seventh-day Adventist home in England. From an early age he was drawn to Scripture and to the great questions of theology: Who is God? How does He govern? Why is there evil? As a young man he moved to the United States, completed advanced degrees in New Testament and theology, and eventually joined the faculty at Loma Linda University, where he taught for decades. But Maxwell was never content with the academic guild alone. He loved the church, loved teaching Sabbath School, and loved sitting in living rooms fielding questions from laypeople who wanted to understand their Bibles better.

What made Maxwell distinctive was his relentless focus on a single question: Can God be trusted? For him, this was not an abstract theological puzzle but the heart of the great controversy between Christ and Satan. He taught that Lucifer's rebellion began with accusations against God's character‚Äîthat God was arbitrary, selfish, and ruling by fear. These lies fractured heaven and then infected humanity in Eden. Ever since, the central drama of history has been God's patient, non-coercive effort to win back the trust of His creatures by revealing the truth about Himself.

Maxwell insisted that the cross is not a legal transaction to satisfy wrath but a revelation of who God is. At Calvary, the universe sees that God is exactly as He claimed: self-giving, faithful, and non-arbitrary. The Father does not vent anger on the Son; rather, God in Christ absorbs the consequences of our rebellion and invites us back into friendship. Atonement, for Maxwell, is reconciliation‚ÄîGod setting things right by demonstrating His trustworthiness so compellingly that hearts are won and accusations are silenced.

The Larger View of Everything

Maxwell's teaching method was conversational and cumulative. He would begin with a simple question‚ÄîWhy does God allow suffering? What is the purpose of the law? What does judgment mean?‚Äîand then unfold a "larger view" that reframed the question entirely. The law, he explained, is not a test to see if we can obey but a revelation of God's character meant to inspire trust. Judgment is not God looking for reasons to condemn but God transparently demonstrating that He has been fair and patient, so the universe can see the truth. Final punishment is not eternal torture but the tragic self-destruction of those who refuse to trust, allowed by a God who will not coerce.

Throughout his life, Maxwell emphasized that God seeks friends, not slaves. Obedience rooted in fear is worthless; only love-born trust produces genuine loyalty. This vision transformed how many Adventists understood their faith. Instead of a religion centered on keeping rules to avoid wrath, they discovered a gospel centered on knowing and trusting a God whose character is revealed supremely in the cross. Maxwell's warmth, humility, and clarity made this "larger view" feel like coming home.

How this page reflects Maxwell's influence
  • The framing of the great controversy as a trust issue rather than a power struggle comes directly from Maxwell.
  • The emphasis on God governing by persuasion, not force echoes his teaching on divine government.
  • The insistence that the cross is revelation, not transaction mirrors his atonement theology.
  • The call to reinterpret law, judgment, and punishment through the lens of God's trustworthiness reflects his "larger view" method.

To ask "Can God be trusted?" and to answer with the cross, to see judgment as disclosure rather than condemnation, and to understand God's government as persuasion rather than force is to stand in the tradition Graham Maxwell spent his life cultivating with patience, clarity, and love.
