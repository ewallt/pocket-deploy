God’s Reputation and the Great Controversy

The issue is not divine power but divine trustworthiness.

Maxwell frames Scripture as a long dispute over God’s character. From Eden onward, Satan has accused the Creator of being unjust and untrustworthy. Every story in the Bible is part of that controversy, revealing how God answers the charges — not by force, but by truth.

God’s reputation determines the destiny of the universe. If His creatures doubt His fairness, love disintegrates and fear takes its place. To resolve this tension, God does not silence His critics; He invites examination of His ways.

The cross becomes the decisive evidence. There, divine justice and mercy are seen in harmony, exposing the lies that made obedience seem dangerous. Christ’s death was not payment to appease wrath but revelation to restore confidence in God’s heart.

For Maxwell, salvation is relational, not transactional. Eternal life depends on knowing God as He truly is. The plan of redemption, therefore, is a campaign of communication — the truth about God told clearly at infinite cost.

When God’s character is vindicated, the great controversy ends. All creation will once again serve Him, not as fearful subjects, but as joyful friends.

Sources: *Servants or Friends?*, Ch. 4 “God’s Reputation and the Great Controversy.”
