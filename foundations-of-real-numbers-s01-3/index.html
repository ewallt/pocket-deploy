Cauchy Sequences and Convergence without Guesswork

Identifying limits by name demands foresight: one must guess the candidate and then prove the approach. Cauchy reframed the matter internally by inspecting the sequence itself. If terms eventually cluster arbitrarily tightly, convergence is declared to occur, whether or not we can name the destination. In the rationals, Cauchy sequences need not have rational limits; that defect exposes incompleteness. In the reals, every Cauchy sequence converges, and that closure under approximations makes analysis operational.

This viewpoint liberates proofs from explicit formulas. Rather than solving for a limit first, we verify that approximations stabilize under the chosen metric, and the system guarantees the limit’s existence. The shift is conceptual: convergence becomes a property of behavior rather than recognition. With that move, results such as completeness of ℝ, the Bolzano–Weierstrass theorem, and the uniform limit theorem acquire clean, modular proofs grounded in internal criteria.
