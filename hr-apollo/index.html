The Apollo Program

1961 – 1972 • One Giant Leap For Mankind • Knowledge Base

To The Moon and Back

The Apollo program was the third United States human spaceflight program carried out by NASA, which succeeded in preparing and landing the first humans on the Moon from 1968 to 1972. It was first conceived during the Eisenhower administration as a three-person spacecraft to follow the one-person Project Mercury, which put the first Americans in space.

The Command Module Crew

Click on a crew member to see their role:

Commander (CDR)

The mission commander was responsible for the overall mission, the crew, and the safety of the vehicle. The commander was the first person to step out of the Lunar Module onto the Moon's surface.

Command Module Pilot (CMP)

The CMP remained in lunar orbit aboard the Command/Service Module while the other two astronauts landed on the Moon. They performed crucial orbital science experiments and were responsible for the rendezvous and docking with the returning Lunar Module.

Lunar Module Pilot (LMP)

The LMP was the co-pilot of the Lunar Module and was responsible for its systems during the descent, landing, and ascent. The LMP was the second person to walk on the Moon.

Milestone Missions

Apollo 1 (Jan 1967)

Tragedy on the Launchpad

A cabin fire during a launch rehearsal claimed the lives of the three-man crew: Gus Grissom, Ed White, and Roger B. Chaffee. The tragedy led to extensive redesigns of the Command Module, significantly improving its safety.

Apollo 8 (Dec 1968)

First to Orbit the Moon

The first crewed mission to leave low Earth orbit and orbit the Moon. The crew of Frank Borman, Jim Lovell, and William Anders broadcast a famous Christmas Eve message and took the iconic "Earthrise" photograph.

Apollo 11 (Jul 1969)

The Eagle Has Landed

The first mission to land humans on the Moon. Neil Armstrong and Buzz Aldrin spent over 21 hours on the lunar surface while Michael Collins orbited above.

Apollo 13 (Apr 1970)

A Successful Failure

An in-flight explosion of an oxygen tank crippled the Service Module, forcing the crew to use the Lunar Module as a lifeboat to return to Earth safely. The mission is hailed as a triumph of ingenuity and perseverance.

Apollo 17 (Dec 1972)

The Final Mission

The last mission of the Apollo program. It included the first scientist (geologist Harrison Schmitt) to walk on the Moon and marked the end of the initial era of human lunar exploration.

Apollo 11: One Giant Leap

The Crew

Commander: Neil A. Armstrong

Command Module Pilot: Michael Collins

Lunar Module Pilot: Edwin "Buzz" E. Aldrin, Jr.

Launch Date: July 16, 1969
The Landing

On July 20, 1969, the Lunar Module "Eagle" landed in the Sea of Tranquility. Armstrong famously reported, "Houston, Tranquility Base here. The Eagle has landed."

During the final descent, Armstrong took manual control to pilot the LM away from a hazardous, boulder-filled crater, landing with only seconds of fuel remaining.

First Steps

Six hours after landing, Neil Armstrong became the first human to step onto the Moon's surface, declaring, "That's one small step for [a] man, one giant leap for mankind."

Buzz Aldrin joined him about 20 minutes later.

Surface Activities

The two astronauts spent about 2.5 hours outside the LM. They collected lunar samples, planted the U.S. flag, and deployed scientific instruments, including a seismometer and a laser ranging retroreflector.

Time on Surface: 21 hours, 36 minutes

The Technology of Apollo

Saturn V Rocket

The most powerful rocket ever successfully flown. Standing over 363 feet tall, the three-stage rocket was necessary to propel the heavy Apollo spacecraft out of Earth's orbit and towards the Moon.

Command/Service Module (CSM)

The "mother ship" of the Apollo spacecraft. The Command Module housed the crew for the journey, and the Service Module provided propulsion, power, and life support. The CM was the only part to return to Earth.

Lunar Module (LM)

A two-stage vehicle designed solely for operation in space. The descent stage landed on the Moon, and the ascent stage returned the astronauts to orbit to rendezvous with the CSM.

Apollo Guidance Computer (AGC)

One of the first computers to use integrated circuits. The AGC handled complex calculations for navigation, guidance, and control of the spacecraft, a revolutionary feat for its time.
