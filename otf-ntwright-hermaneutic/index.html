N.T. Wright's Hermeneutic: An Exploration

Overview of Wright's Hermeneutic

N.T. Wright proposes a hermeneutical method called "Critical Realism" to interpret biblical texts. This approach seeks a middle path between naive positivism (the idea that we can know objective reality perfectly) and postmodern phenomenalism (the idea that we can only know our own sense-data). It acknowledges that our knowledge is always provisional and shaped by our worldview, yet insists that we can still make true claims about a reality that exists independently of us.

  • Integrative Method: Wright combines worldview analysis, narrative criticism, and historical research into a cohesive framework.
  • Worldview as Key: Understanding the "worldview" of a text—its underlying story, symbols, and answers to ultimate questions—is fundamental to interpretation.
  • History as Interpretation: All historical knowledge is an interpretation, arrived at by testing hypotheses against available data, not by accessing "bare facts".

N.T. Wright, a prominent New Testament scholar, has developed a comprehensive interpretive framework that has significantly influenced modern Protestant theology. His project, particularly the "Christian Origins and the Question of God" series, is notable for its ambitious scope, attempting to provide a full account of New Testament history and theology.

The foundation of his method is an epistemology known as Critical Realism. Borrowed from the philosophy of science, this view accepts that we can never have direct, unmediated access to reality ("the thing-in-itself") and that all our knowledge is theory-laden. However, it rejects the conclusion that this prevents us from making meaningful and reality-depicting claims. For Wright, this means we can genuinely know things about the historical Jesus and the first-century world, even if that knowledge is always open to revision.

This epistemological stance leads him to prioritize the concept of worldview. He argues that texts are articulations of worldviews, best understood as grand stories. To interpret a text like the Gospels, the reader must first discern the story of Second-Temple Judaism that the text affirms, subverts, or redefines. This "macro-hermeneutic" of analyzing the overarching narrative provides the context for a "micro-hermeneutic" that examines smaller units of text using refined versions of form and redaction criticism.

Core Concepts

Wright's hermeneutic is built on several key interlocking concepts. Critical Realism provides the epistemological foundation, while Worldview Analysis offers the primary interpretive lens. Narrative and history are not separate categories but are deeply intertwined, with story being the main vehicle for articulating a worldview and understanding historical intentionality.

  • Critical Realism: Acknowledges that reality exists independently of the knower, but our access to it is always mediated through dialogue and interpretation.
  • Worldview Components: Worldviews are expressed through four interconnected elements: stories, symbols, ultimate questions, and praxis (a way of being).
  • Double Similarity/Dissimilarity: A key criterion for historical authenticity, suggesting that genuine sayings of Jesus should be both credibly situated within first-century Judaism and credibly the starting point for early Christianity, yet distinct from both.

Critical Realism is an epistemological thesis that forges a middle path between the certainty of positivism and the skepticism of phenomenalism. It asserts two main points:

  • Realism: It acknowledges the reality of the thing known, as something other than the knower. There is an objective world "out there."
  • Critical: It fully acknowledges that our only access to this reality is through a "spiralling path of appropriate dialogue or conversation between the knower and the thing known." All knowledge is mediated, provisional, and subject to revision.

In historical research, this means that while we cannot observe the past directly, we can form hypotheses and test them against data to arrive at genuine, albeit provisional, knowledge about historical events and their meaning.

For Wright, worldviews are the foundational stories that shape a culture's perception of reality. They are expressed through four interconnected components:

  1. Stories: The narratives that order one's view of reality (e.g., the story of Israel's exile and restoration). This is the most comprehensive expression.
  2. Symbols: Shorthand statements or objects that point to the larger story (e.g., the Temple, Torah).
  3. Ultimate Questions: The implicit answers a worldview provides to questions like: Who are we? Where are we? What is wrong? What is the solution?
  4. Praxis: The way of being in the world; the practices and behaviors that embody the worldview.

Interpreting a text requires discerning the worldview it articulates by analyzing these components within the text itself, rather than imposing an external framework.

Wright rejects the idea of "mere history" or "bare facts." All historical writing is an act of interpretation, involving selection and a judgment of what is meaningful. The goal of the historian is to move from event to meaning, which he defines as understanding the "intentionalities of the characters concerned."

He distinguishes between:

  • Aim: The fundamental direction of a person's life.
  • Intention: The specific application of that aim in a particular situation.
  • Motivation: The immediate sense that a specific action is appropriate to accomplish an intention.

By analyzing a historical figure's words and actions (praxis) against the backdrop of their worldview, a historian can reasonably deduce their aims and intentions without claiming to know their private psychological state.

Critiques and Implications

While influential, Wright's hermeneutical framework is not without its critics. Debates often center on whether his "critical realism" successfully escapes the pitfalls of older forms of realism or if it sufficiently accounts for postmodern critiques of truth and knowledge. The implications of his method are significant, calling for a re-evaluation of how historical, literary, and theological inquiries are conducted in biblical studies.

  • Epistemological Concerns: Some scholars argue that "critical realism" may still hide a "pretentious aspiration to 'get it right'," underestimating the depth of postmodern challenges.
  • Integration of Methods: The successful fusion of narrative analysis (typically ahistorical) with historical-critical research is a point of discussion and evaluation.
  • Reframing Biblical Authority: Wright's model of authority as an "unfinished play" challenges traditional views, framing Scripture as a guide for faithful improvisation rather than a rulebook.

Is "Critical Realism" a Cover for Old Realism?

J. Richard Middleton and Brian J. Walsh express concern that the term "critical realism" carries "too many overtones of a realism that has proven to be bankrupt." They suggest it could be a "kinder and gentler" version of realism that still harbors an aspiration to achieve a final, universally true perspective. They fear this can lead to a "totalizing stance that is idolatrous in character" and propose a narrative approach to truth that is not tied to any form of realism.

Narrative Theology and Critical Inquiry

Other scholars, however, see no inherent conflict. Van Huyssteen argues that narrative approaches are compatible with critical realism as long as the narrative remains open to critique from other sources. A distinction is made between "pure" narrative theologies (which resist external philosophical analysis) and "impure" ones (which invite it). Wright's approach clearly falls into the "impure" category, as he uses philosophical concepts like critical realism and historical methods to analyze and ground the biblical narrative. He advocates for starting with the biblical story but does not discourage philosophical or systematic reflection upon it.

Theological Reading as a Necessary Component

A key implication of Wright's method is the insistence on integrating historical, literary, and theological readings. He argues that a purely historical reading loses personal relevance, while a purely literary ("what it means to me") reading loses public relevance. A theological reading is necessary because the worldviews expressed in the texts are inherently theological. He asserts that "theological language... [is] on the same footing as language about anything else" and is a valid dimension for discourse about reality, subject to the same public critique and correction.

Glossary of Terms

Critical Realism
An epistemological position that acknowledges an objective reality while also acknowledging that our access to it is always mediated, partial, and subject to revision.
Double Similarity and Dissimilarity
A criterion for historical authenticity which posits that a saying or story of Jesus is likely authentic if it is credibly rooted in first-century Judaism and credibly the origin of an early Christian belief, yet is not identical to either.
Hermeneutics
The theory and methodology of interpretation, especially the interpretation of biblical texts, wisdom literature, and philosophical texts.
Phenomenalism
The view that we can only be sure of our own sense-data and cannot know of the existence of an external reality that causes it. Wright critiques this as often leading to solipsism.
Positivism
A philosophical theory stating that certain knowledge is based on natural phenomena and their properties and relations. In Wright's usage, a "naïve" view that one can simply and objectively look at reality.
Praxis
A way of being in the world; the practices, habits, and actions that embody and express a particular worldview. Crucial for determining the aims and intentions of historical figures.
Worldview
A comprehensive conception or apprehension of the world, especially from a specific standpoint. For Wright, it is primarily expressed through foundational stories, symbols, answers to ultimate questions, and praxis.
