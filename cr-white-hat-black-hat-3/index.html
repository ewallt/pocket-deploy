The Lie Behind the Lens

*Stories don’t just entertain; they catechize.*

Western TV taught a generation how to feel about justice. Tight pacing compresses complexity into a duel; moral debate resolves at high noon. The camera trains our instincts: quick violence reads as courage, and hesitation reads as weakness. The method hides in the motion, smuggling a creed past our guard.

The creed says: outcomes are what matter. If the town is safe, the means are justified. Once this settles into our bones, we carry it to worship and politics, to parenting and mission. We become pragmatic about righteousness, confident that a good cause sanctifies a dark tactic.

The gospel unmasks this pedagogy. Truth is not a statistic to be maximized but a Person to be trusted. When ends and means diverge, we do not win—we become something else. The Father’s kingdom advances by persuasion, patience, and self-giving love or it does not advance at all.

To see the lie is to slow down the scene. Freeze the frame where the “hero” deceives, humiliates, or kills, and ask: would Jesus do this? If not, then the hat color has misled us. Righteousness cannot be filmed in black-hat techniques and remain righteous.

Stories will keep teaching. So we choose better ones—or read old ones differently—until our imagination matches the character of Christ and we recognize virtue by its methods, not its soundtrack.

Sources: Behold Your God, “Magnifying the Law” (culture as moral formation).
