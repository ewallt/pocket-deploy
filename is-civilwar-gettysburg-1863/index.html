Gettysburg (1863)

The High-Water Mark of the Confederacy

Core Summary

From July 1-3, 1863, Robert E. Lee's Army of Northern Virginia clashed with George Meade's Army of the Potomac near Gettysburg, Pennsylvania. A chance encounter on July 1 escalated into the war's largest battle. Union forces occupied the high ground‚ÄîCemetery Hill, Cemetery Ridge, and Culp's Hill. Lee attacked both flanks on July 2, nearly breaking through at Little Round Top. On July 3, 12,500 Confederates under George Pickett charged across open ground toward Cemetery Ridge. Union artillery and rifle fire shattered the assault. Lee retreated to Virginia, never again able to threaten Northern territory. Combined with Vicksburg's fall the same day, Gettysburg marked the war's turning point.

Lee's Invasion: After Chancellorsville, Lee invaded Pennsylvania to threaten Northern cities, influence 1864 elections, and win European recognition. His army marched north flush with confidence.
Meeting Engagement: Neither commander planned to fight at Gettysburg. Union cavalry discovered Confederate forces approaching. Both armies converged on the crossroads town. Lee lacked Stuart's cavalry for reconnaissance.
The High Ground: Union forces retreated through town to Cemetery Hill on July 1. General Winfield Scott Hancock rallied troops and secured the position. Meade brought the Army of the Potomac to this strong defensive line.

ü™ñ Forces & Commanders

‚ñº
Union: Meade commanded approximately 93,500 troops. He had taken command only three days before the battle. Corps commanders included Hancock, Reynolds (killed July 1), and Sickles.
Confederate: Lee commanded approximately 71,000 troops. Longstreet commanded the I Corps, Ewell the II Corps, A.P. Hill the III Corps. Lee sorely missed Jackson's aggressive instinct.
Casualties: Union: 3,155 killed, 14,531 wounded, 5,369 missing. Confederate: 4,708 killed, 12,693 wounded, 5,830 missing. Total: approximately 46,286 casualties‚Äîthe war's bloodiest battle.

üìÖ Timeline

‚ñº
July 1: Confederate forces drive Union troops through Gettysburg. Union I and XI Corps suffer heavy casualties. Troops rally on Cemetery Hill as darkness falls. Lee orders Ewell to take the hill 'if practicable.' Ewell hesitates.
July 2: Lee attacks both Union flanks. Longstreet assaults the left at the Peach Orchard, Wheat Field, and Little Round Top. Colonel Joshua Chamberlain's 20th Maine holds Little Round Top with desperate bayonet charge. Ewell attacks Culp's Hill and Cemetery Hill. All attacks fail.
July 3, morning: Union forces retake positions on Culp's Hill after fierce fighting. Lee plans massive assault on Union center.
July 3, 1 PM: Confederate artillery bombards Cemetery Ridge. Union guns respond then fall silent to conserve ammunition. Lee believes them silenced.
July 3, 3 PM: Pickett's Charge: 12,500 Confederates march across three-quarters of a mile of open ground. Union artillery reopens, tearing gaps in advancing lines. Those reaching the stone wall are killed or captured. The assault fails utterly.

‚ö° Turning Points

‚ñº
Ewell's Hesitation: On July 1, Lee ordered Ewell to take Cemetery Hill 'if practicable.' Ewell judged it impracticable. Had Jackson lived, he might have seized the hill, changing the battle's outcome.
Little Round Top: If Confederates had captured this key position, they could have enfiladed the Union line. Chamberlain's defense and Warren's quick thinking saved Meade's left flank.
Pickett's Charge: Lee's decision to assault the Union center remains controversial. Longstreet protested it was doomed. The charge's failure sealed Lee's defeat and destroyed Confederate offensive capacity.

üìä Outcomes

‚ñº
Lee's Retreat: On July 4, Lee's army began retreating in driving rain. Meade pursued cautiously. Lincoln urged aggressive pursuit to destroy Lee's army but Meade let the opportunity pass.
Turning Point: Combined with Vicksburg's fall July 4, Gettysburg marked the war's strategic shift. Confederate armies would never again threaten Northern territory. The South fought defensively thereafter.
Lincoln's Address: Four months later, Lincoln delivered the Gettysburg Address at the battlefield cemetery dedication, redefining the war's purpose as preserving government 'of the people, by the people, for the people.'

üîç Historical Significance

‚ñº

Gettysburg stands as the Civil War's most famous battle and America's bloodiest. Historian James McPherson calls it the war's 'great turning point.' Lee's defeat ended Confederate strategic initiative. Never again would Southern armies invade Northern territory. The three-day battle produced more casualties than any other Civil War engagement. Pickett's Charge became synonymous with futile valor. Lee's army escaped but was never the same. Losses among veteran officers and troops could not be replaced. Gettysburg proved Lee could be beaten in open battle on favorable ground. Combined with Grant's Vicksburg triumph, it marked July 1863 as the Confederacy's high-water mark. From Gettysburg forward, Southern strategy shifted from winning independence through military victory to holding out long enough for Northern war-weariness to bring negotiated peace.
