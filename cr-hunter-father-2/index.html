Misread Motives

*An overheard conversation can sound like approval when it’s actually rescue.*

Imagine a passerby hearing the father instruct his son about rifles—cleaning, loading, aim, trigger discipline. Without context, the lesson sounds like advocacy. “He’s a hunting enthusiast,” the stranger concludes, unaware that every sentence was spoken to prevent a tragedy.

This is how Scripture is often misread. We overhear God giving regulations about kings, war, divorce, or sacrifice and assume He endorses the systems those rules govern. In truth, He is speaking into choices already made, shaping them toward the least harm and the most hope.

Permission is not prescription. Concession is not command. The Father’s aim is unchanged: to form a people who love life and each other. When our stubbornness narrows His options, He uses the space we allow to keep us alive and reachable.

Accommodation will always risk slander. Those who do not know the heart will cite the rules as evidence of complicity. Those who know the heart will recognize mercy disguised as management—saving involvement where rejection would have been simpler.

To understand God rightly, we must supply the missing context: a Father staying close to a child bent on danger. Only then do His words sound like what they are—rescue, not approval.

Sources: Deuteronomy 24; 1 Samuel 8; Matthew 19:8; Behold Your God (divine accommodation).
