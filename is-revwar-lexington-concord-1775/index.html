Lexington & Concord (1775)

The Shot Heard Round the World

Core Summary

On April 19, 1775, British regulars marched from Boston to seize colonial military supplies in Concord. Warned by Paul Revere and William Dawes, local militia confronted the British at Lexington Green. The resulting skirmish marked the beginning of armed conflict between Britain and its American colonies. As the British withdrew to Boston, militia harassed them along the road, inflicting heavy casualties and demonstrating that colonial resistance would not be easily suppressed.

British Objectives: General Thomas Gage aimed to confiscate colonial weapons and arrest revolutionary leaders Samuel Adams and John Hancock.
Colonial Situation: Tensions had escalated after the Boston Tea Party and the Coercive Acts. Militias had been training and stockpiling arms in response to British authority.
Intelligence Networks: Dr. Joseph Warren's spy network detected British movements. The midnight rides warned the countryside that regulars were marching.

British: Lt. Col. Francis Smith commanded 700 regulars from the 4th, 5th, 10th, 23rd, 38th, 43rd, 47th, 52nd, and 59th Regiments of Foot, plus Royal Marines.
Colonial: Captain John Parker led 77 militiamen at Lexington. Colonel James Barrett commanded roughly 400 militia at Concord. Thousands more joined the running battle back to Boston.
Casualties: British suffered 73 killed, 174 wounded, 53 missing. Colonials: 49 killed, 39 wounded, 5 missing.

The Running Battle: The British withdrawal became a tactical disaster. Militia used terrain and local knowledge to inflict heavy casualties, demonstrating the effectiveness of irregular warfare.

üìä Outcomes

‚ñº
Military: British regulars barely made it back to Boston. The expedition failed to secure significant weapons or arrest leaders. Militia besieged Boston immediately after.
Political: News of Lexington and Concord spread throughout the colonies, galvanizing support for armed resistance and making reconciliation with Britain far more difficult.
Psychological: The battles shattered the myth of British invincibility. Colonial militia proved they could stand against professional soldiers.

üîç Historical Significance

‚ñº

Lexington and Concord mark the point of no return in the American Revolution. What had been a political and economic dispute became a shooting war. Ralph Waldo Emerson's phrase "the shot heard round the world" captures how this local skirmish reverberated globally, ultimately leading to American independence and inspiring revolutionary movements worldwide.
